“Autosomes” are chromosomes that are not sex chromosomes (e.g. not X or Y chromosomes). Somatic cells are all the cells in an organism except for the gametes (egg and sperm cells) and the cells that produce the gametes. In a diploid organism, such as a human, somatic cells have two sets of chromosomes and gametes have only one set. Human somatic cells have 22 pairs of autosomes and one pair of sex chromosomes. Gametes have only one set of autosomes and one sex chromosome. Sperm cells can have either an X or a Y chromosome for their sex chromosome. In contrast, all egg cells have an X chromosome. A zygote, which results from fertilization of an egg cell by a sperm cell, has 22 pairs of autosomes (so 44 autosomes) and two sex chromosomes, as do somatic cells of a male.

During meiosis I, one of each pair of homologous chromosomes ends up in each daughter cells. So, at the completion of meiosis I the resulting daughter cells have half as much DNA and half as many chromosomes as a cell that has replicated its DNA and is about to begin meiosis. At the completion of meiosis II, the pair of sister chromatids making up each chromosome separate, with each daughter cell receiving one of the chromatids, which are each now independent chromosomes. As a result, the number of chromosomes per cell does not decrease during meiosis II, but the amount of DNA per cell is again cut in half. Therefore, the overall process of meiosis I and II results in a 4-fold reduction in the amount of DNA per cell and a 2-fold reduction in the number of chromosomes per cell.

A diploid organism has two sets of chromosomes, meaning that it has two copies of each gene in its somatic cells (remember that somatic cells are all the cells in an organism, except for the gametes and the cells that produce the gametes). Slightly different versions of the same gene are known as “alleles”. So, for each gene, a diploid organism can have either two copies of the same allele or two different alleles of that gene. During meiosis, the pairs of homologous chromosomes separate and the daughter cells that result at the end of meiosis have only one set of chromosomes and therefore only one allele of each gene. In this example, the female in question has different alleles of the wing color gene but the same (short) allele of the antennae length gene. Therefore, half the egg cells produced by this female will have one copy of the blue allele of the wing color gene and the other half of the egg cells will have one copy of the orange allele. Because this female only has the short allele of the antennae length gene, all the egg cells produced by this female will have one copy of the short allele of the antennae length gene on chromosome 19.

The amounts of cyclin “cycle” during the cell cycle, and are higher during the part of the cell cycle when they act. Somatic cells are all the cells in an organism, except for the gametes and the cells that give rise to the gametes. The amount of DNA per somatic cells doubles during S phase, as the cells replicate their DNA. The amount of DNA remains at this level throughout G2 phase, and then is cut in half part way through mitosis as the cell divides and each of the resulting daughter cells receives half of the DNA. So, somatic cells in G1 and G0 (remember that G0 is the phase that G1 cells enter if they don’t pass the checkpoint at the end of G1 that would allow them to enter the S phase) have half as much DNA as somatic cells during G2 and the first part of mitosis. During meiosis, two rounds of cell division occur with only one round of DNA synthesis. As a result, the gametes formed by meiosis have only half as much DNA as a somatic cell during the G1 or G0 phases, and only a quarter as much DNA as a somatic cell during G2 or the earlier part of mitosis. Therefore, sample I represents somatic cells in G1, G0 or the last part of mitosis. Sample II represents somatic cells in G2 or the earlier part of mitosis. Sample III represents gametes.

Sister chromatids separate during anaphase of both mitosis and meiosis II. DNA replication occurs prior to meiosis I, but not before meiosis II. Because two rounds of cell division but only one round of DNA replication occurs during meiosis, the cells produced at the end of meiosis are haploid. In contrast, during mitosis the cell undergoes both one round of DNA synthesis and one round of cell division. Therefore, the daughter cells produced by mitosis are diploid and have the same chromosome number as the starting cell.

The amount of DNA per chromosome is not affected by tissue type. However, the amount of DNA per chromosome will be twice as high in cells where the DNA has replicated but the resulting sister chromatids are still together (i.e. part of the same chromosome) than in cells where sister chromatids have separated. For example, in G1 phase the DNA has not yet replicated. In contrast, G2
phase occurs after DNA synthesis but before mitosis. So, during G2
phase each chromosome consists of two sister chromatids that are stuck together at the centromere. Therefore, chromosomes in cells in G2 phase have twice as much DNA as chromosomes in cells in G1 phase. Prior to telophase the sister chromatids separate, becoming independent chromosomes. Consequently the amount of DNA per chromosome for cells in telophase is the same as for cells in G1, but half as much as for cells in G2.
