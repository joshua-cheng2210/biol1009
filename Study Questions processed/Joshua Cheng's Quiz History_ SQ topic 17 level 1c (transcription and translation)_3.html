The template strand of a DNA molecule is the strand that is hydrogen bonded to complementary ribonucleotides during the formation of an RNA molecule. So, the sequence of the RNA molecule will be complementary to the sequence of the DNA template strand.  Also, remember that nucleotide chains base pair with an anti-parallel arrangement, meaning that the 5 end of one molecule is base paired with the 3 end of the other molecule, and vice versa.  Finally, remember that RNA molecules contain U in place of T.  So, if the template strand is 
 
template strand:                5 TTTAAGG 3
complementary strand (RNA):     3 AAAUUCC 5
 
If we now write the sequence of the RNA molecule in the more normal 5 to 3 orientation (in other words, if we now write the sequence of the RNA molecule backwards to what it is above), we see that the sequence of the RNA molecule will be:  5 CCUUAAA 3

When the ribosome reaches a stop codon on the mRNA, the stop codon will be aligned with the A (aminoacyl) site of the ribosome. Normally, a tRNA with an appropriate anticodon binds the mRNA codon that is aligned with the A site of the ribosome. However, when the codon that is aligned with the A site is a stop codon, a protein called a release factor will bind the stop codon in the A site instead of a tRNA. When this happens, the polypeptide chain attached to the tRNA in the ribosome P site is cleaved from the tRNA and the translation assembly (e.g. the ribosome, tRNA and mRNA) comes apart.

Promoters are DNA sequences at the beginnings of genes where transcription factors and RNA polymerase bind to form an initiation complex. Transcription begins as the transcription complex moves along the mRNA, from the promoter towards the coding region of the gene. So, promoter sequences are not transcribed and so are also not translated to form a protein. Start and stop codons play important roles in initiating and terminating TRANSLATION, rather than TRANSCRIPTION. Aminoacyl tRNA synthetases play important roles during translation, rather than transcription.

When we say that the genetic code is almost universal, what we mean is that almost all organisms use the same codons to specify the same amino acids. In other words, ATG will specify the amino acid methionine in almost every organism. Because of this, a gene that is transferred from one species to another will almost always code for a protein of the same amino acid sequence in the new species as in the original species from which the gene came. When we say that the genetic code is redundant, what we mean is that most amino acids are specified by more than one codon. For example, there are six different codons that all specify the amino acid leucine. When we say that the genetic code is non-ambiguous, what we mean is that each codon specifies only one amino acid. So, multiple codons can specify the same amino acid, but a given codon only specifies a single amino acid. When we say that the genetic code exhibits wobble, what we mean is that the anticodons of some tRNAs are able to base pair with multiple codons that have almost identical sequences. In cases where a tRNA can base pair with multiple codons, each of the codons the tRNA can base pair with will specify the same amino acid. So, wobble does NOT cause the genetic code to be ambiguous. A particular tRNA will always help insert the same amino acid into a polypeptide, its just that the tRNA may act at more than one codon that specifies that particular amino acid.

During elongation of a polypeptide, when a new, charged tRNA enters the ribosome, it enters at the A (amino acyl) site of the ribosome. At that point in time, the tRNA to which the growing polypeptide is attached is in the P (peptidyl tRNA) site of the ribosome. After the new, charged tRNA enters the A site of the ribosome, the ribosome catalyzes the breaking of the covalent bond connecting the polypeptide with the tRNA in the P site and then catalyzes to formation of a new covalent bond between the polypeptide and the amino acid attached to the tRNA in the A site. After that happens, the ribosome shifts position along the mRNA molecule such that the tRNA that was in the P site is now in the E site, and the tRNA that was in the A site is now in the P site. The tRNA that is now in the E (exit) site now leaves the ribosome. A new, charged tRNA enters the A site, and the whole process is repeated.

The template strand of a gene is the DNA strand to which ribonucleotides hydrogen bond during formation of an RNA molecule. Consequently, RNA molecules will have a nucleotide sequence that is complementary to the nucleotide sequence of the DNA template strand.  The other DNA strand of a gene is called the coding strand.  The coding strand will be complementary to the template strand.  So, both the coding strand of the gene and the RNA made from that gene will be complementary to the template strand of the gene.  This means that the coding strand of a gene and the RNA made from that gene will have the same nucleotide sequence, with the exception that U will be substituted for T in the RNA molecule.
So, if the sequence of the coding strand of the DNA molecule is: 5 ATGCGTA 3
The nucleotide sequence of the RNA molecule made from this segment of DNA will be: 5 AUGCGUA 3
