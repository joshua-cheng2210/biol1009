Plants that are true breeding are homozygous for the gene that controls the character being studied, meaning that they have two copies of the same allele of that gene. The fact that all the F1 plants have red, axial flowers means that the red allele of the flower-color gene is dominant and the axial allele of the flower-type gene is dominant. If we represent the red and white alleles of the flower color gene as R and r, respectively, and the axial and terminal alleles of the flower type gene as T
and t, respectively, the cross the original parent plants was: RRTT

Good work!

The dominant trait is W. Affected individuals are represented by dark squares or circles and unaffected individuals are represented by open squares or circles. An individual (I-2) who is Ww is shown as being affected. Thus, we know that the dominant allele, W, causes individuals to be affected. Because IV-4 is not affected, IV-4 must be ww. IV-3 is affected, which could be due to being WW or Ww. We can determine that IV-3 must be Ww, because IV-3 has a parent who is not affected. This parent must therefore be ww, meaning that IV-3 must have inherited a w allele from that parent. Therefore, the cross is: Ww X ww. This cross will result in an equal mixture of progeny that are Ww (and are affected) and ww (and are not affected). Therefore, 50% of the progeny of IV-3 and IV-4 will be affected.

Nice job!

A monohybrid cross is a cross between two organisms that are both heterozygous for a particular genetic locus (e.g., gene). For example, Bb X Bb would be a monohybrid cross as both individuals are heterozygous for a single locus (the B locus) that is being studied. A dihybrid cross is a cross between two organisms that are both heterozygous as the same two loci (e.g., genes). For example, if height is controlled by the H locus and seed color is controlled by the C locus, then the following would be an example of a dihybrid cross: BbCc X BbCc. You would expect to the progeny of a monohybrid cross to exhibit a 3:1 phenotypic ratio (3 individuals exhibiting the dominant phenotype for every one individual exhibiting the recessive phenotype). The progeny of a dihybrid cross should exhibit a 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio (9 individuals exhibiting both dominant phenotypes, for every 3 individuals exhibiting the dominant phenotype for trait one and the recessive phenotype for trait two, for every 3 individuals exhibiting the recessive phenotype for trait one and the dominant phenotype for trait two, for every one individual exhibiting the recessive phenotypes for both traits).
