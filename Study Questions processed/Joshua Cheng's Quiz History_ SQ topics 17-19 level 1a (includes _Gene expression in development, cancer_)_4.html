The WORST mutations that were listed are the deletion of 1, 2 or 4 nucleotides. The reason for this is that such mutations will cause what is known as a frame shift. A frame shift mutation causes all the codons (words) to be read incorrectly. For example, if a particular DNA sequence has codons as follows: ATG AGG CCC TTT, removing the first two nucleotides would change the codons to: GAG GCC CTT etc. These frame shifted codons would specify different amino acids than the non frame shifted codons, so that the amino acid sequence of the polypeptide made from the mutated gene would be almost completely different than the amino acid sequence of the polypeptide made from a gene that is not mutated. Removing 1 or 4 nucleotides will also cause a frame shift mutation. In contrast, deleting 3 consecutive nucleotides will not cause a frame shift, and so the mutated gene will produce a polypeptide that is almost identical in sequence to the polypeptide produced by the unmutated gene, but will just be missing one amino acid, or will have one amino acid that is substituted for two different amino acids (depending on which 3 nucleotides are deleted). Such a small change in amino acid sequence may have only a small affect on the folding of the polypeptide into the correct 3-dimensional shape, and the polypeptide may still be able to carry out most or all of its normal functions.

If codons were four nucleotides long, any of the four nucleotides (A, C, G or T/U) could occupy the 1st position of the codon and any of the four nucleotides could occupy the 2nd, 3rd and 4th
positions of the codon.  The total number of codons that could thus be formed would be: 4 x 4 x 4 x 4 = 256, meaning that a maximum of 256 different amino acids could be specified if codons were four nucleotides long.  Because there are actually only 20 amino acids, codons do not need to be four nucleotides long to specify all of them (i.e. codons only need to be 3 nucleotides long to be able to specify all 20 amino acids).

The normal function of tumor-suppressor genes is to decrease the rates of cell division and/or growth, or to function in DNA repair or cell adhesion.  Mutations in tumor-suppressor genes that act in DNA repair can raise the chances of a cell becoming cancerous because that cell will tend to have an increased number of mutations.  Some of those extra mutations may occur in proto-oncogenes, converting them to oncogenes, or in tumor-suppressor genes, decreasing the protection against cancer normally provided by tumor-suppressor genes.
 
The normal function of proto-oncogenes is to produce polypeptides that increase the rate of cell division and/or growth.  Mutations that increase the expression of a proto-oncogene can turn it into an oncogene that tends to promote excessive rates of cell division and/or growth and that increases the chances of a cell becoming cancerous.

Normally, when the ribosome reaches a stop codon on the mRNA, the stop codon will be aligned with the A (aminoacyl) site of the ribosome. A protein called a release factor will bind the stop codon in the A site. When this happens, the polypeptide chain attached to the tRNA in the ribosome P site is cleaved from the tRNA and the translation assembly (e.g. the ribosome, tRNA and mRNA) comes apart. In this experiment, this process was arrested, meaning that no release factor was allowed to enter the A site. As a consequence, the polypeptide will still be attached to the tRNA in the P site and the ribosome will not have dissociated from the mRNA molecule.

Three nucleotides are always used to code for each amino acid. However, this fact cannot explain why a transcript that is 8,000 nucleotides long is using 1,200 of those nucleotides to make a protein consisting of approximately 400 amino acids. The fact that 1,200 nucleotides can be used to make a protein that is 400 amino acids long makes sense, since it takes 3 nucleotides to specify each amino acid and 1,200/3 = 400. However, it does not explain why the transcript is 8,000 nucleotides long, but only 1,200 nucleotides are being used to specify amino acids. What are the other 6,800 nucleotides doing? The answer is that they are introns (intervening sequences) that are being excised from the pre-mRNA during formation of the mature mRNA and that it is the mature mRNA that is translated to form polypeptides.

In eukaryotes, transcription units are usually significantly longer than mature mRNAs. This is due to the presence of noncoding DNA sequences, known as intervening sequences, or introns. These introns are removed from precursor mRNA (pre-mRNA, also known as primary mRNA) molecules by splicing.
