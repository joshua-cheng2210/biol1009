Chromatin (the mixture of DNA and proteins, such as histones, complexed with the DNA that forms eukaryotic chromosomes) is only found in eukaryotic cells.  Changes to the chromatin of eukaryotic cells include changes both to the proteins that form part of chromatin (e.g. acetylation of histone proteins) as well as to the DNA itself (e.g. DNA methylation, which means adding methyl groups to specific nucleotides).  Note that when we talk about changes in chromatin structure we are NOT taking about changes in the actual nucleotide sequence of the DNA.

The lac repressor gene encodes the lac repressor protein. The ability of the repressor gene to encode the repressor protein wont be affected by moving the gene, with its promoter, to a different position on the chromosome. The normal function of tumor-suppressor genes is to decrease the rates of cell division and/or growth, or to function in DNA repair or cell adhesion.  Mutations in tumor-suppressor genes that act in DNA repair can raise the chances of a cell becoming cancerous because that cell will tend to have an increased number of mutations.  Some of those extra mutations may occur in proto-oncogenes, converting them to oncogenes, or in tumor-suppressor genes, decreasing the protection against cancer normally provided by tumor-suppressor genes.
 
The normal function of proto-oncogenes is to produce polypeptides that increase the rate of cell division and/or growth.  Mutations that increase the expression of a proto-oncogene can turn it into an oncogene that tends to promote excessive rates of cell division and/or growth and that increases the chances of a cell becoming cancerous.

If codons were two nucleotides long, any of the four nucleotides (A, C, G or T/U) could occupy the 1st position of the codon and any of the four nucleotides could occupy the 2nd position of the codon.  The total number of codons that could thus be formed would be: 4 x 4 = 16, meaning that a maximum of 16 different amino acids could be specified if codons were only two nucleotides long.  Because there are actually 20 amino acids, codons need to be more than two nucleotides long to specify all of them (i.e. codons need to be 3 nucleotides long to be able to specify all 20 amino acids).
