Early during embryo development in females, the XIST gene becomes active on one of the two X chromosomes. The X chromosome on which the XIST gene becomes active is random, and is different for different cells in the embryo. When the XIST gene becomes active, it is transcribed and its transcript (RNA) attaches to the X chromosome that made it. Interaction of the RNA from the XIST gene initiates inactivation of that X chromosome. The inactive X chromosome condenses, forming what is known as a Barr body.

All somatic cells in humans who are biologically female have two X chromosomes. So that biological females don't produce twice as much protein from genes on the X chromosome as biological males, during development a random X chromosome in each cell is inactivated in biological females. Because which of the two X chromosomes is inactivated is random for every cell, in some cells the X chromosome that was inherited from the biological father is inactivated whereas in other cells the X chromosome that was inherited from the biological mother is inactivated. As a result, different cells have different X chromosomes inactivated, causing biological females to be genetic mosaics.
