The allele for brown eyes (B) is found within a population at a frequency of 0.7; the allele for blue eyes (b) at a frequency of 0.3. If the conditions for the Hardy-Weinberg law are satisfied, what will be the expected frequency of heterozygous (Bb) individuals?

If the conditions of the Hardy-Weinberg law are satisfied, that means that the frequencies of the alleles and genotypes in a population will remain constant from generation to generation, provided that only Mendelian segregation and recombination of alleles are at work. In other words, nothing such as sexual selection is occurring.

That means that the frequency of different genotypes in the population can be expressed as: p2 + 2pq + q2 = 1, where p is the frequency of the dominant allele (0.7 in this example), q is the frequency of the recessive allele (0.3 in this example) and p2 therefore represents the fraction of individuals who are homozygous for the dominant allele, 2pq represents the fraction of individuals who are heterozygous and q2 represents the fraction of individuals who are homozygous for the recessive allele.

In this example, that means the frequency of heterozygous (Bb) individuals = 2pq = 2 x 0.7 X 0.3 = 0.42.
