Good work!

Plants that are “true breeding” are homozygous for the gene that controls the character being studied, meaning that they have two copies of the same allele of that gene. The fact that all the F1 plants have red, axial flowers means that the red allele of the flower-color gene is dominant and the axial allele of the flower-type gene is dominant. If we represent the red and white alleles of the flower color gene as R and r, respectively, and the axial and terminal alleles of the flower type gene as T
and t, respectively, the cross the original parent plants was: RRTT
x rrtt. Therefore, the all the F1 plants will be RrTt. A cross between two F1 plants (RrTt X RrTt) will yield an equal mix of F2 progeny with the following genotypes for the flower color gene: RR, Rr, rR and rr. The ¼ of the plants that are rr will have white flowers. The cross between two F1
plants will yield an equal mix of F2 progeny with the following genotypes for the flower-type gene: TT, Tt, tT and tt. The ¾ of the plants that are TT, Tt or tT will have axial flowers. Therefore, ¼ X ¾ = 3/16 of the plants will have white axial flowers.

Good job!

The wild-type alleles of both genes are represented by “+”. The cross can be written as follows: +bt+vg X +bt+vg. For the BT gene, there will be an equal mixture of progeny with each of the following genotypes: ++, +b, b+ and bb. Because the mutant allele is recessive, only the ¼ of the progeny that are bb will have bent wings. Of the ¼ of the progeny that have bent wings, ¼ will also be homozygous for the mutant allele of the VG gene, and thus will also have vestigial wings. In other words, ¼ X ¼ of the progeny will have both bent and vestigial wings. The probability of offspring with bent wings only is therefore: 1/4 - 1/16 = 3/16.

Good job!

Early during embryo development in females, the XIST gene becomes active on one of the two X chromosomes. The X chromosome on which the XIST gene becomes active is random, and is different for different cells in the embryo. When the XIST gene becomes active, it is transcribed and its transcript (RNA) attaches to the X chromosome that made it. Interaction of the RNA from the XIST gene initiates inactivation of that X chromosome. The inactive X chromosome condenses, forming what is known as a “Barr body”.

No. Try making Punnett squares for each locus and use the genotypic frequencies of offspring to calculate the probabilities for each locus.

To obtain the overall probability; multiply the individual probabilities for each locus. For example, all of the offspring will inherit a copy of “A” from the first parent and ½ will inherit a copy of “A” from the second parent and ½ will inherit a copy of “a” from the second parent. So, ½ the offspring will be AA and ½ will be Aa. Similarly, ½ the offspring will be BB and ½ will be Bb. For the third gene, all the offspring will inherit a copy of “c” from the first parent and all will inherit a copy of “C” from the second parent, so all the offspring will be Cc. For the 4th gene, ½ the offspring will inherit “D” and ½ will inherit “d” from the 1st
parent, and ½ the offspring will inherit “D” and ½ will inherit “d” from the 2nd parent. As a result, the offspring will consist of an equal mixture of: DD, Dd, dD and dd, meaning that ¼ of the offspring will be dd. Similarly, the probability of an offspring being EE is also ¼. Because all the offspring will inherit a copy of “F” from parent 1, and all the offspring will inherit a copy of “f” from parent 2, there is a 100% probability that their offspring will be Ff. So, if you multiply all of these probabilities, you get: (1/2)(1/2)(1)(1/4)(1/4)(1) = 1/64.
