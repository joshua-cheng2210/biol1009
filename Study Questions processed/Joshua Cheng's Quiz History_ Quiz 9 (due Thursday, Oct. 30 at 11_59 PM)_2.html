This result suggests that both parents are heterozygous for the trait. If the dominant allele of the trait is “D”, and the recessive allele is “d”, that means both parents are Dd and produce an equal mixture of progeny with the following genotypes (= allele combinations): DD, Dd, dD and dd. The first three genotypes will produce the dominant phenotype (observable trait) and the fourth will produce the recessive phenotype. Therefore, the offspring will have a 3:1 ratio for the trait. If one of the parents was homozygous, you would see a different ratio of traits among the offspring. For example, if one parent was DD and the other was Dd, the offspring would be an equal mix of DD and Dd. In that case, all the progeny would exhibit the dominant trait. If one parent was dd and the other was Dd, half the progeny would be dd and the other half would be Dd. In that case, ½ the progeny would exhibit the dominant trait and the other ½ would exhibit the recessive trait.

A monohybrid cross is a cross between two organisms that are both heterozygous for a particular genetic locus (e.g., gene). For example, Bb X Bb would be a monohybrid cross as both individuals are heterozygous for a single locus (the B locus) that is being studied. A dihybrid cross is a cross between two organisms that are both heterozygous as the same two loci (e.g., genes). For example, if height is controlled by the “H” locus and seed color is controlled by the “C” locus, then the following would be an example of a dihybrid cross: BbCc X BbCc. You would expect to the progeny of a monohybrid cross to exhibit a 3:1 phenotypic ratio (3 individuals exhibiting the dominant phenotype for every one individual exhibiting the recessive phenotype). The progeny of a dihybrid cross should exhibit a 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio (9 individuals exhibiting both dominant phenotypes, for every 3 individuals exhibiting the dominant phenotype for trait one and the recessive phenotype for trait two, for every 3 individuals exhibiting the recessive phenotype for trait one and the dominant phenotype for trait two, for every one individual exhibiting the recessive phenotypes for both traits).

For most of his experiments, Mendel crossed two individuals that were each homozygous for different alleles of a particular character. For example, he crossed plants that were homozygous for the purple (P) allele of the flower color gene with plants that were homozygous for the white (p) allele of the flower color gene. All the F1 progeny thus had the Pp genotype and all had purple flowers, because the purple allele is dominant. If Mendel had stopped with the F1 generation, he would not have known whether the plants simply didn’t inherit the white allele, or whether the effects of the white allele were masked by the presence of a purple allele. By crossing two F1 plants that were Pp, he got a 1:2:1 ratio of F2
plants with the following genotypes: PP, Pp and pp. The pp plants had white flowers, demonstrating that the white allele wasn’t lost, its effect was simply masked by the presence of the purple allele.
