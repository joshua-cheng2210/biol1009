A monohybrid cross is a cross between two organisms that are both heterozygous for a particular genetic locus (e.g., gene). For example, Bb X Bb would be a monohybrid cross as both individuals are heterozygous for a single locus (the B locus) that is being studied. A dihybrid cross is a cross between two organisms that are both heterozygous as the same two loci (e.g., genes). For example, if height is controlled by the “H” locus and seed color is controlled by the “C” locus, then the following would be an example of a dihybrid cross: BbCc X BbCc. You would expect to the progeny of a monohybrid cross to exhibit a 3:1 phenotypic ratio (3 individuals exhibiting the dominant phenotype for every one individual exhibiting the recessive phenotype). The progeny of a dihybrid cross should exhibit a 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio (9 individuals exhibiting both dominant phenotypes, for every 3 individuals exhibiting the dominant phenotype for trait one and the recessive phenotype for trait two, for every 3 individuals exhibiting the recessive phenotype for trait one and the dominant phenotype for trait two, for every one individual exhibiting the recessive phenotypes for both traits).

The animal is diploid, and thus has two copies of each chromosome. One copy of one of its chromosomes carries an H allele, and the homologous copy of that chromosome carries an h allele of the gene for head shape. A different chromosome carries a T allele, and the homologous chromosome for that chromosome carries a t allele, for the tail length gene. The gametes produced by this animal will be haploid, meaning that they will have only one set of chromosomes, and therefore only one allele of each gene. The gametes produced could have any of the following genotypes: HT, Ht, hT or ht.

They can NOT be tt, Hh or HhTt, because then they would have two copies of the tail length gene and no copies of the head shape gene (if tt), two copies of the head shape gene and no copies of the tail length gene (if Hh), or two copies of both genes (if HhTt).

Mendel studied alleles where one allele was completely dominant to the other. For example, the allele of the flower color gene that causes a purple color is completely dominant to the allele that causes a white color. So, progeny that inherited two purple alleles or one purple allele and one white allele looked exactly like the parent that had two purple alleles or a purple allele and a white allele (i.e. they all had purple flowers). Progeny that inherited two white alleles had white flowers, just like the parent that had two white alleles.

Before Mendel’s work, many people thought that the traits exhibited by progeny were a result of the “blending” of the traits of the parents. Mendel’s work showed that this is not true. Instead, we inherit genes from our parents that determine our traits. In many cases one allele of a particular gene is dominant to another allele, so that progeny only exhibit the trait of the dominant allele. For example, children who inherit one allele for blue eyes and one allele for brown eyes have brown eyes, not eyes that are halfway between blue and brown.
