Prior to DNA replication, the cell has four chromosomes. Each of those chromosomes will replicate during S phase, forming four pairs of sister chromatids. During anaphase, separase cleaves the cohesin proteins holding each of the four pairs of sister chromatids together, allowing the sister chromatids to separate and become eight independent chromosomes. By telophase (which occurs immediately after anaphase), each daughter nucleus will have received one copy of each chromosome, meaning that each of the daughter nuclei will have 4, separate chromosomes.

Many plant and fungal species make use of both asexual and sexual reproduction. Asexual reproduction occurs without fertilization (e.g. by mitosis). It is true that during sexual reproduction parents transmit 50% of their genes to their offspring. This happens because during the process of meiosis the parents produce germ cells (either sperm or egg cells) each of which contain only 50% of the DNA present in the somatic (non-germ) cells of the parent. However, sexual reproduction does NOT require that the parents be diploid. For example, if the parents are tetraploid (i.e. contain four sets of chromosomes), meiosis will result in the production of germ cells that are diploid (i.e. contain two sets of chromosomes).
