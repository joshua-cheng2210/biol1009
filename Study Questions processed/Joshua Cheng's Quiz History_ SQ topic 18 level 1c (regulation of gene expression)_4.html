âOperonsâ are clusters of genes that are under the control of a single regulatory sequence (e.g. a promoter sequence).  Removing an operon would thus result in the genes of that operon being off permanently, rather than being unable to be turned off.  Similarly, promoters are nucleotide sequences near genes where RNA polymerase binds and starts the process of transcribing the gene(s) to form an RNA molecule. 
 
When trying to remember the difference between inducers and corepressors of repressor proteins, remember that these small molecules are NOT named because of how they affect the activity of the repressor protein.  Instead, they are named because of how they affect the rate of transcription of the genes in the operon(s) they help regulate.  So, âinducersâ are called inducers because scientists used to think that they âinduceâ (i.e. âturn onâ) expression of the genes in the operons they help regulate. We now know that what inducers actually do is to stabilize repressor proteins in their inactive form.  By stabilizing repressor proteins in their inactive form, inducers keep the repressor proteins from binding to the operator sequences and preventing transcription.  So, in a sense, inducers allow gene transcription to proceed by turning off a repressor.   Corepressors work in kind of the opposite way.  Corepressors stabilize the repressor proteins with which they interact in their active form.  So, when the correct corepressor is present in a cell, the repressor protein with which it interacts will bind to it and be stabilized in its active form.  The active repressor/corepressor complex will then bind the operator sequence, preventing transcription of the genes in the operon by RNA polymerase.  So, the name âcorepressorâ refers to the fact that corepressors help repress transcription of the genes in the operon.
