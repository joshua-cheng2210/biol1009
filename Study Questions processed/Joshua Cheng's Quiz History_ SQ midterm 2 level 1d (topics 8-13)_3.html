Remember that electrons have more energy, the further they are from the nucleus.  So, when an electron moves closer to the nucleus, that electron loses (releases) energy.  Also, remember that when something is “reduced”, what that means is that its charge is reduced.  Because electrons are negatively charged, when an atom gains an electron or an electron moves closer to an atom, the atom is “reduced” because it’s charge is reduced.

Catabolic pathways tend to produce ATP from ADP whereas anabolic pathways tend to produce ADP from ATP.  If ATP levels in the cell are high, it makes sense to slow down catabolic pathways as the cell doesn’t need to be producing more ATP at that point.  As a consequence, many catabolic enzymes evolved so that they are inhibited by ATP.  For a similar reason, many catabolic enzymes are activated by ADP.  So, if ATP levels in the cell are low and ADP levels are high, you would expect catabolic reactions to proceed quickly.

If you have trouble remembering the difference between catabolism and anabolism, picture a cat tearing something up to remember that catabolism is the breakdown of large, energy-rich molecules to produce smaller, less energy-rich molecules.  Anabolism is the opposite (i.e. production of large, energy-rich molecules from smaller, less complex and less energy-rich molecules).

Some of the energy released during electron transport is used to do the work of forming a proton (i.e. hydrogen ion) gradient).  Some of the energy stored in that gradient is then used to do the work of forming ATP, meaning that some of the energy stored in the gradient is converted to energy stored in the form of covalent bonds in the ATP molecule.  Remember that not all of the energy released during transport of electrons down the electron transport chain can be used to form a proton gradient, as that would be a violation of the 2nd law of thermodynamics.  According to this law, whenever energy is transferred or transformed, the amount of entropy in the universe decreases and, consequently, the amount of energy in the universe that is able to do work decreases.  So, only some of the energy released during electron transport can be used to do the work of building a proton gradient.

A diploid organism has two sets of chromosomes, meaning that it has two copies of each gene in its somatic cells (remember that somatic cells are all the cells in an organism, except for the gametes and the cells that produce the gametes). Slightly different versions of the same gene are known as “alleles”. So, for each gene, a diploid organism can have either two copies of the same allele or two different alleles of that gene. During meiosis, the pairs of homologous chromosomes separate and the daughter cells that result at the end of meiosis have only one set of chromosomes and therefore only one allele of each gene. In this example, the female in question has different alleles (blue and orange) of the wing color gene and different alleles (short and long) of the antennae length gene. Therefore, half the egg cells produced by this female will have one copy of the blue allele of the wing color gene and the other half of the egg cells will have one copy of the orange allele. In addition, half the egg cells produced by this female will have one copy of the short allele of the antennae length gene and the other half of the egg cells will have one copy of the long allele of the antennae length gene. Because the wing color and antennae length genes are on different chromosomes, which allele of the wing color gene is inherited by a particular egg cell will not be affected by which allele of the antennae length gene is inherited by that egg cell. As a result, half of the cells will inherit the orange allele of the wing color gene and half of those cells (or ¼ of the total daughter cells) will inherit the short allele of the antennae length gene and the other half of those cells (or ¼ of the total daughter cells) will inherit the long allele of the antennae length gene. Similarly, half of the cells will inherit the blue allele of the wing color gene and half of those cells (or ¼ of the total daughter cells) will inherit the short allele of the antennae length gene and the other half of those cells (or ¼ of the total daughter cells) will inherit the long allele of the antennae length gene.
