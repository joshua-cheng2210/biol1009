When a new, charged tRNA enters the ribosome, it enters at the A (amino acyl) site of the ribosome. At that point in time, the tRNA to which the growing polypeptide is attached is in the P (peptidyl tRNA) site of the ribosome. After the new, charged tRNA enters the A site of the ribosome, the ribosome catalyzes the breaking of the covalent bond connecting the polypeptide with the tRNA in the P site and then catalyzes to formation of a new covalent bond between the polypeptide and the amino acid attached to the tRNA in the A site. After that happens, the ribosome shifts position along the mRNA molecule such that the tRNA that was in the P site is now in the E site, and the tRNA that was in the A site is now in the P site. The tRNA that is now in the E (exit) site now leaves the ribosome. A new, charged tRNA enters the A site, and the whole process is repeated.

Normally operator sequences lie between promoter sequences (where RNA polymerase binds) and the structural genes of the operon.  This arrangement means that, if active repressor protein is bound to the operator sequence, it will physically block the movement of RNA polymerase along the mRNA molecule towards the structural genes of the operon.  So, the normal placement of the operator sequence between the promoter sequence and the structural genes of the operon is necessary for its function.  If you move the operator sequence so that it is on the far side of the structural genes from the promoter, even if active repressor protein binds the operator sequence, it wont be in the right place to be able to block transcription of the structural genes.

Operons are clusters of genes that are under the control of a single regulatory sequence (e.g. a promoter sequence).  Operons are very common in prokaryotes but are rare in eukaryotes.  Operons contain multiple genes, which must all be located next to each other so that all of the genes in the operon can be transcribed from a single promoter (so the genes that make up a particular operon can NOT be on different chromosomes).  Although there are operons that contain genes involved in lactose metabolism, lac
