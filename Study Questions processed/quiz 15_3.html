The pH of a solution is equal to the negative logarithm (base 10) of the hydrogen ion concentration in moles per liter.  In other words, pH = - log [H+].  So, for example:

If the pH = 1, than:

1 = - log [H+].  Or, -1 = log [H+].  Or, 10-1 = [H+]. Or, 0.1 moles/liter (M) = [H+]

and

If the pH = 2, than:

2 = - log [H+].  Or, -2 = log [H+].  Or, 10-2 = [H+]. Or, 0.01 moles/liter (M) = [H+].

So, when the pH of a solution decreases from 2 to 1, the hydrogen ion concentration in the solution increases by 10 times.  Similarly, when the pH decreases from 7.4 to 6.4, the hydrogen ion concentration increases by 10 times.

Trophic levels are levels in the food chain. In a lake, the first trophic level is composed of cyanobacteria, which produce organic material through photosynthesis. The second trophic level is composed of zooplankton, which eat cyanobacteria. The third trophic level is composed of fish and other organisms that eat zooplankton.
