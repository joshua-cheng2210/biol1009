For most of his experiments, Mendel crossed two individuals that were each homozygous for different alleles of a particular character. For example, he crossed plants that were homozygous for the purple (P) allele of the flower color gene with plants that were homozygous for the white (p) allele of the flower color gene. All the F1 progeny thus had the Pp genotype and all had purple flowers, because the purple allele is dominant. If Mendel had stopped with the F1 generation, he would not have known whether the plants simply didnt inherit the white allele, or whether the effects of the white allele were masked by the presence of a purple allele. By crossing two F1 plants that were Pp, he got a 1:2:1 ratio of F2
plants with the following genotypes: PP, Pp and pp. The pp plants had white flowers, demonstrating that the white allele wasnt lost, its effect was simply masked by the presence of the purple allele.

If the tall allele is represented as T and the short allele is represented by t, the cross is: Tt X TT. Half of the progeny will be TT and half of the progeny will be Tt. Because all the progeny will have at least one T allele, and the T allele is dominant and causes the plants to be tall, all of the offspring will be tall. Therefore, there is 0 probability of this cross producing a short plant.

This question asks you to consider 5 genes, labeled A through E. The individual in question has the same allele for two of the genes. In other words, the individual has two copies of the C allele and two copies of the E allele. So, all the gametes produced by this individual will inherit a copy of the C allele of the C gene and the E allele of the E gene. In contrast, the individual has two different alleles of each of the other 3 genes. For each of those three genes, a gamete can inherit either the dominant allele (shown with an upper-case letter) or the recessive allele (shown with a lower-case letter). So, the possible combinations of the alleles for those three genes are: ABD, ABd, AbD, Abd, aBD, aBd, abD and abd. So, this individual can produce gametes with 8 different genotypes.

A monohybrid cross is a cross between two organisms that are both heterozygous for a particular genetic locus (e.g., gene). For example, Bb X Bb would be a monohybrid cross as both individuals are heterozygous for a single locus (the B locus) that is being studied. A dihybrid cross is a cross between two organisms that are both heterozygous as the same two loci (e.g., genes). For example, if height is controlled by the H locus and seed color is controlled by the C locus, then the following would be an example of a dihybrid cross: BbCc X BbCc. You would expect to the progeny of a monohybrid cross to exhibit a 3:1 phenotypic ratio (3 individuals exhibiting the dominant phenotype for every one individual exhibiting the recessive phenotype). The progeny of a dihybrid cross should exhibit a 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio (9 individuals exhibiting both dominant phenotypes, for every 3 individuals exhibiting the dominant phenotype for trait one and the recessive phenotype for trait two, for every 3 individuals exhibiting the recessive phenotype for trait one and the dominant phenotype for trait two, for every one individual exhibiting the recessive phenotypes for both traits).

The wild-type alleles of both genes are represented by +. The cross can be written as follows: +bt+vg X +bt+vg. For the BT gene, there will be an equal mixture of progeny with each of the following genotypes: ++, +b, b+ and bb. In other words, X of the progeny will have both bent and vestigial wings. The probability of offspring with bent wings only is therefore: 1/4 - 1/16 = 3/16.
