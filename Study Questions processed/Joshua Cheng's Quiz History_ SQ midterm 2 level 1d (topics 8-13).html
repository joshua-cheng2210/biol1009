Nice work!

During the energy investment phase, two molecules of ATP are used to create a sugar molecule with two phosphate groups attached. Because the bonds between phosphate groups and the rest of a molecule are high-energy bonds, this di-phosphorylated molecule is therefore a high-energy molecule. The high energy possessed by the di-phosphorylated molecule allows it to split into two molecules, each of which has three carbon atoms. As these three-carbon molecules are metabolized to form pyruvate, some of the energy that is released is used to form ATP, in the energy payoff phase of glycolysis.

Good job!

Catabolic pathways tend to produce ATP from ADP whereas anabolic pathways tend to produce ADP from ATP.  If ATP levels in the cell are high, it makes sense to slow down catabolic pathways as the cell doesnt need to be producing more ATP at that point.  As a consequence, many catabolic enzymes evolved so that they are inhibited by ATP (and activated by ADP).  This is an example of feedback inhibition, as a product of the catabolic pathway, namely ATP, is inhibiting enzymes that catalyze early steps in the catabolic pathway.  Similarly, many anabolic enzymes evolved so that they are inhibited by ADP (and activated by ATP).  So, ADP (not ATP) acts as a feedback inhibitor of anabolic pathways.


If you have trouble remembering the difference between catabolism and anabolism, picture a cat tearing something up to remember that catabolism is the breakdown of large, energy-rich molecules to produce smaller, less energy-rich molecules.  Anabolism is the opposite (i.e. production of large, energy-rich molecules from smaller, less complex and less energy-rich molecules).

To remember the difference between endergonic and exergonic, focus on the first part of each word.  With endergonic, think of energy entering the system as the free energies of the products are greater than the free energies of the reactants. With exergonic, think of energy exiting the system as the free energies of the products are less than the free energies of the reactants.  Also remember that transfer of electrons along an electron transport chain is from molecules that are less electronegative (want electrons less) to molecules that are more electronegative (want electrons less) and is thus an energetically favorable, or exergonic, process.
