Alternative splicing is a mechanism that is commonly used by eukaryotes to allow their genes to produce multiple, related, proteins.  Approximately 95% of human genes that have more than one exon are thought to make use of alternative splicing.  For example, the troponin T gene has 5 exons.  All 5 exons, plus the introns between the exons, are present in each primary RNA transcript made from the gene.  However, different primary RNA transcripts from this gene are spliced in slightly different ways (i.e. the undergo alternative splicing).  Some of the primary RNA transcript molecules are spliced such that the mature mRNA transcript has exons 1, 2, 3 and 5.  Other primary RNA transcript molecules are spliced such that the mature mRNA transcript has exons 1, 2, 4 and 5.

When the ribosome reaches a stop codon on the mRNA, the stop codon will be aligned with the A (aminoacyl) site of the ribosome. Normally, a tRNA with an appropriate anticodon binds the mRNA codon that is aligned with the A site of the ribosome. However, when the codon that is aligned with the A site is a stop codon, a protein called a release factor will bind the stop codon in the A site instead of a tRNA. When this happens, the polypeptide chain attached to the tRNA in the ribosome P site is cleaved from the tRNA and the translation assembly (e.g. the ribosome, tRNA and mRNA) comes apart.

When a new, charged tRNA enters the ribosome, it enters at the A (amino acyl) site of the ribosome. At that point in time, the tRNA to which the growing polypeptide is attached is in the P (peptidyl tRNA) site of the ribosome. After the new, charged tRNA enters the A site of the ribosome, the ribosome catalyzes the breaking of the covalent bond connecting the polypeptide with the tRNA in the P site and then catalyzes to formation of a new covalent bond between the polypeptide and the amino acid attached to the tRNA in the A site. After that happens, the ribosome shifts position along the mRNA molecule such that the tRNA that was in the P site is now in the E site, and the tRNA that was in the A site is now in the P site. The tRNA that is now in the E (exit) site now leaves the ribosome. The A site of the ribosome is now lined up with the codon specifying the next amino acid that needs to be added to the polypeptide and a new, charged tRNA enters the A site, and the whole process is repeated.
