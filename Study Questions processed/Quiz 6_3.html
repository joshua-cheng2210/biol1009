Remember that, although exergonic reactions are also known as spontaneous reactions, the fact that a reaction is spontaneous does not mean that it will occur quickly or even that it will occur at all.  Exergonic, or spontaneous, reactions are defined as reactions where the free energies of the reactant molecules are higher than the free energies of the product molecules.  So, if the reaction occurs, there will be a net decrease in free energy.  HOWEVER, for a reaction to occur the chemical bonds in the reactant molecules must be stretched into higher energy forms, so that the reactant molecules reach what is known as a transition state.  The free energies of the reactant molecules in their transition states are higher than the free energies of the reactant molecules in their non-transition states.  So, the reactant molecules may not have sufficient energy to achieve their transition states.  If that is the case, the reaction will not proceed even though the overall effect of the reaction would be a decrease in free energy.  Note that adding energy to some of the reactant molecules, so that those molecules are able to undergo the reaction, will result in a release of energy that may result in more reactant molecules having sufficient energy to surmount the activation energy barrier and undergo the reaction, leading to a further release in energy that allows more reactant molecules to undergo the reaction, and so on.  In other words, once a reaction gets going, it may keep going.

For a reaction to be a spontaneous reaction, the free energy of the reactant molecules must be greater than the free energy of the product molecules, so that free energy decreases as a result of the reaction.  So, if you imagine a reaction where A is converted to B, the conversion of a molecule of A to a molecule of B will be spontaneous if the free energy of molecule A is greater than the free energy of molecule B.  So, if on average A molecules have a free energy of 5 kcal/mole and B molecules have a free energy of 4.9 kcal/mole, the average free energy of a molecule of A will be greater than the average free energy of a molecule of B and therefore the conversion of an average molecule of A to an average molecule of B will represent an exergonic, or spontaneous, reaction.  However, what we have to realize is that different molecules of A in a solution will have slightly different free energies and different molecules of B will have slightly free different energies.  So, if the greater than 5 kcal/mole can then be converted to molecules of A via a reaction that will be exergonic (spontaneous).  Note that this does NOT mean that the conversion of B to A will occur at as high a rate as the conversion of A to B.  If there are only a very small number of molecules of B that have a high enough free energy level to be converted to A via an exergonic reaction, the rate of conversion of A to B will be higher than the rate of conversion of B to A.  Eventually, the high rate of conversion of A to B will result in there being enough molecules of B with sufficient free energy to be converted to A via an exergonic reaction that the rate of conversion of B to A will equal the rate of conversion of A to B.  At that point, the reaction will have reached equilibrium and
