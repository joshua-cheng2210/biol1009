For most of his experiments, Mendel crossed two individuals that were each homozygous for different alleles of a particular character. For example, he crossed plants that were homozygous for the purple (P) allele of the flower color gene with plants that were homozygous for the white (p) allele of the flower color gene. All the F1 progeny thus had the Pp genotype and all had purple flowers, because the purple allele is dominant. If Mendel had stopped with the F1 generation, he would not have known whether the plants simply didnt inherit the white allele, or whether the effects of the white allele were masked by the presence of a purple allele. By crossing two F1 plants that were Pp, he got a 1:2:1 ratio of F2
plants with the following genotypes: PP, Pp and pp. The pp plants had white flowers, demonstrating that the white allele wasnt lost, its effect was simply masked by the presence of the purple allele.

Before Mendels work, many people thought that the traits exhibited by progeny were a result of the blending of the traits of the parents. Mendels work showed that this is not true. Instead, we inherit genes from our parents that determine our traits. In many cases one allele of a particular gene is dominant to another allele, so that progeny only exhibit the trait of the dominant allele. For example, children who inherit one allele for blue eyes and one allele for brown eyes have brown eyes, not eyes that are halfway between blue and brown.

The fact that a cross between radishes with red and white flowers yields all-purple offspring tells us that the red and white alleles of the flower color gene must exhibit incomplete dominance, so that a red/white heterozygote has a phenotype intermediate to that of red/red or white/white homozygotes. Therefore, all the F1 progeny from a cross between a plant that is true breeding (homozygous) for the red allele and a plant that is homozygous for the white allele will be heterozygous for the flower color gene, and therefore purple. In contrast, the long allele of the radish shape gene is dominant over the oval allele. Therefore, a cross between a plant that is homozygous for the long allele and a plant that is homozygous for the oval allele will result in all F1 progeny being heterozygous for the radish shape gene, and thus having long radishes. Therefore, all the F1
progeny will have long, purple radishes.

The animal is diploid, and thus has two copies of each chromosome. One copy of one of its chromosomes carries an H allele, and the homologous copy of that chromosome carries an h allele of the gene for head shape. A different chromosome carries a T allele, and the homologous chromosome for that chromosome carries a t allele, for the tail length gene. The gametes produced by this animal will be haploid, meaning that they will have only one set of chromosomes, and therefore only one allele of each gene. The gametes produced could have any of the following genotypes: HT, Ht, hT or ht.

They can NOT be tt, Hh or HhTt, because then they would have two copies of the tail length gene and no copies of the head shape gene (if tt), two copies of the head shape gene and no copies of the tail length gene (if Hh), or two copies of both genes (if HhTt).

Dominant alleles are represented by upper case letters. The first parent has at least one dominant allele for each gene, and therefore exhibits the dominant phenotype for each gene. Progeny that also have at least one dominant allele for all three genes will also exhibit the dominant phenotype for each gene and so will look like the first parent. If we consider each gene separately, AA
is being crossed with Aa. The progeny will thus be an equal mixture of AA
and Aa, and all will exhibit the dominant phenotype (trait) for gene A. BB
is being crossed with bb. The progeny will all be Bb and will all exhibit the dominant phenotype for gene B. Cc is being crossed with Cc. The progeny will be an equal mixture of the following genotypes: CC, Cc, cC
and cc. The of the progeny that are CC, Cc or cC
will all exhibit the dominant phenotype for gene C. Therefore, of the progeny will exhibit the dominant phenotype for all three genes.
