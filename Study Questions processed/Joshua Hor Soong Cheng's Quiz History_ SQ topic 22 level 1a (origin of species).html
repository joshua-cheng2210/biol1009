Good job!

Although speciation often occurs very slowly and involves a large number of genetic changes, it can occur very quickly and involve only a single genetic change. Although speciation often occurs in isolated populations, it can also occur in populations that are not geographically isolated from each other. For example, mistakes in cell division can lead to an organism being polyploid (having more than 2 sets of chromosomes). Polyploid organisms are not able to produce fertile offspring when crossed with non-polyploid (e.g. diploid) members of the species from which they evolved.

Good job!

According to the biological species concept, a species is a group of populations whose members have the potential to interbreed in nature and produce viable, fertile offspring, but that do not produce viable, fertile offspring with members of other such groups. So, according to this concept, an organism of one species is not able to produce viable, fertile offspring with a member of a different species. Therefore, biological species are reproductively isolated. The biological species concept cannot be used to distinguish species among extinct forms of life, as we generally lack information about which of those forms of life were able to breed with other forms and produce viable, fertile offspring.

According to the phylogenetic species concept, a species is defined as the smallest group of individuals that share a common ancestor, forming one branch of the tree of life. Biologists compare an organism's physical characteristics or molecular sequences to the corresponding data for other species. This analysis can distinguish groups of individuals that are sufficiently different to be considered separate species.
