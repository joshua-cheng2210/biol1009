Typically the mitotic phase is divided into two, partially overlapping sub-phases. Nuclear division occurs during mitosis and division of the cytoplasm to form two daughter cells occurs during cytokinesis. So, if a cell undergoes one round of mitosis without undergoing cytokinesis, that means that the nucleus of that cell divides but the cell itself does not divide, leaving a single cell with two nuclei.

Even if crossing over does not occur, many different chromosomal combinations are possible. For example, if a species has a haploid number of 2 chromosomes and no crossing over occurs, the following 4 combinations are possible for the gametes:
homolog 1 of chromosome 1 + homolog 1 of chromosome 2;
homolog 1 of chromosome 1 + homolog 2 of chromosome 2;
homolog 2 of chromosome 1 + homolog 1 of chromosome 2;
homolog 2 of chromosome 1 + homolog 2 of chromosome 2.

homolog 1 of chromosome 1 + homolog 1 of chromosome 2 + homolog 1 of chromosome 3; homolog 1 of chromosome 1 + homolog 1 of chromosome 2 + homolog 2 of chromosome 3; homolog 1 of chromosome 1 + homolog 2 of chromosome 2 + homolog 1 of chromosome 3; homolog 1 of chromosome 1 + homolog 2 of chromosome 2 + homolog 2 of chromosome 3; homolog 2 of chromosome 1 + homolog 1 of chromosome 2 + homolog 1 of chromosome 3; homolog 2 of chromosome 1 + homolog 1 of chromosome 2 + homolog 2 of chromosome 3; homolog 2 of chromosome 1 + homolog 2 of chromosome 2 + homolog 1 of chromosome 3; homolog 2 of chromosome 1 + homolog 2 of chromosome 2 + homolog 2 of chromosome 3.

A diploid organism has two sets of chromosomes, meaning that it has two copies of each gene in its somatic cells (remember that somatic cells are all the cells in an organism, except for the gametes and the cells that produce the gametes). Slightly different versions of the same gene are known as alleles. So, for each gene, a diploid organism can have either two copies of the same allele or two different alleles of that gene. During meiosis, the pairs of homologous chromosomes separate and the daughter cells that result at the end of meiosis have only one set of chromosomes and therefore only one allele of each gene. In this example, the female in question has two copies of the same allele for both genes being studied. As a result, every gamete (egg cell) produced by that female will have one blue allele of the wing color gene on chromosome 12 and one long allele of the antennae length gene on chromosome 19.

Somatic cells are all the cells in an organism, except for the gametes and the cells that give rise to the gametes. The amount of DNA per somatic cells doubles during S phase, as the cells replicate their DNA. The amount of DNA remains at this level throughout G2 phase, and then is cut in half part way through mitosis as the cell divides and each of the resulting daughter cells receives half of the DNA. So, somatic cells in G1 and G0 (remember that G0 is the phase that G1 cells enter if they dont pass the checkpoint at the end of G1 that would allow them to enter the S phase) have half as much DNA as somatic cells during G2 and the first part of mitosis. During meiosis, two rounds of cell division occur with only one round of DNA synthesis. As a result, the gametes formed by meiosis have only half as much DNA as a somatic cell during the G1 or G0 phases, and only a quarter as much DNA as a somatic cell during G2 or the earlier part of mitosis. Therefore, sample I represents somatic cells in G1, G0 or the last part of mitosis. Sample II represents somatic cells in G2 or the earlier part of mitosis. Sample III represents gametes.
