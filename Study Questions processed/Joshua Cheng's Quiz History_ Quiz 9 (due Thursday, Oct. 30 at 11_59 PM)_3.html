Try drawing a Punnett square to determine the correct answer.

Remember that a heterozygous plant will have copies of two different alleles of the gene in question, whereas a homozygous plant will have two copies of the same allele of the gene in question (either two copies of the dominant allele, or two copies of the recessive allele). One-quarter of the offspring will be homozygous for the dominant allele, one-quarter will be homozygous for the recessive allele, and the remaining half will be heterozygous. For example, in the Punnett square shown below, the dominant allele is represented by “D”, and the recessive allele is represented by “d”. Half of the egg cells will contain “D” and half will contain “d”. Similarly, half of the sperm cells will contain “D” and half will contain “d”. Each egg cell has an equal chance of combining with each sperm cell. Therefore, the probability of any one type of egg cell combining with any one type of sperm cell is ½ X ½ = ¼. Two of these combinations (D X d and d X D) result in production of a heterozygous offspring. So, ¼ + ¼ = ½ of the progeny will be heterozygous.

For most of his experiments, Mendel crossed two individuals that were each homozygous for different alleles of a particular character. For example, he crossed plants that were homozygous for the purple (P) allele of the flower color gene with plants that were homozygous for the white (p) allele of the flower color gene. All the F1 progeny thus had the Pp genotype and all had purple flowers, because the purple allele is dominant. If Mendel had stopped with the F1 generation, he would not have known whether the plants simply didn’t inherit the white allele, or whether the effects of the white allele were masked by the presence of a purple allele. By crossing two F1 plants that were Pp, he got a 1:2:1 ratio of F2
plants with the following genotypes: PP, Pp and pp. The pp plants had white flowers, demonstrating that the white allele wasn’t lost, its effect was simply masked by the presence of the purple allele.

Before Mendel’s work, many people thought that the traits exhibited by progeny were a result of the “blending” of the traits of the parents. Mendel’s work showed that this is not true.
