A component of an electron transport chain will first receive an electron from the component immediately preceding it (i.e. the component that is slightly less electronegative), and will then pass that electron on to the next component in the chain (i.e. the component that is slightly more electronegative than it).  So, each component of an electron transport chain alternates between receiving and donating electrons, and thus between acting as an oxidizing agent (when it receives an electron) and a reducing agent (when it donates an electron, causing the substance to which the electron was donated to have a REDUCED charge).

Actually, this is a true statement.

The statement that is NOT true is that enzymes alter the free energy change of the reactions which they catalyze.  In fact, enzymes do not alter the free energy change of the reactions they catalyze.  Instead, they decrease the activation energy needed for the reaction to occur.  If the activation energy is decreased, more molecules will have enough energy for the reaction to occur and the reaction will occur more quickly.  However, the change in energy for each molecule undergoing the reaction will NOT be affected by the enzyme.  Think of it like this: Imagine a bunch of people trying to jump from a bed, over a dresser, onto the floor.  If the dresser is very high, not too many people will be able to jump over it and make it to the floor.  If you lower the dresser, more people will be able to jump over it and make it to the floor.  However, the decrease in potential energy will be the same regardless of the height of the dresser, since the decrease in potential energy is dependent (in this example) on the difference between the starting height of each person on the bed and their final height on the floor, which is not affected by the height of the dresser.

During meiosis homologous chromosome pairs separate during anaphase I and sister chromatids separate during anaphase II. A mistake in either process could lead to formation of an egg cell with one less chromosome. For example, one pair of homologous chromosomes might not completely separate and so might move towards the same end of the cell during anaphase I. If that happens, one of the resulting cells would not have a copy of either homologous chromosome after cytokinesis I occurs. Alternatively, a pair of sister chromatids might fail to separate and so end up moving to the same end of the cell during anaphase II. If that happens, one of the resulting cells will be not have a copy of either sister chromatid after completion of cytokinesis II. In contrast, if an egg cell isn’t fertilized it will have 23 chromosomes and will not develop into a zygote. Similarly, human egg cells cannot be fertilized by sperm cells from other species.
