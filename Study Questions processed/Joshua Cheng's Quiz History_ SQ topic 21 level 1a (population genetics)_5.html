Prior to the exposure to the antibiotic, different bacterial cells in the population likely had different degrees of resistance to the antibiotic, due to having slightly different versions (alleles) of certain genes. When the antibiotic was applied, those bacteria that were more resistant survived and reproduced at higher rates than the other bacteria. Over time, that meant that a higher and higher percentage of the bacteria in the population would have a high degree of resistance to the antibiotic. This is an example of “directional selection”, which is selection towards one end of a phenotypic spectrum. In this case, the phenotypic spectrum is different degrees of antibiotic resistance and the end of the spectrum that is being selected for is the end that is the most resistant.

If two alleles for a particular locus (e.g. gene) are represented by “A” and “a”, then heterozygotes (Aa) may arise from a sperm cell of genotype A
combining with an egg cell of genotype a or by a sperm cell of genotype a
combining with an egg cell of genotype A. So, if the frequency of allele A is represented by p and the frequency of allele a is represented by q, then the frequency with which Aa heterozygotes arise is: p X q + q X p = 2pq.

“q” represents the frequency of the recessive allele. Individuals showing the recessive trait must have two copies of the recessive allele. The frequency at which such individuals occur will be q X q, or q2. In this example, we are told that 1% of the individuals in a population show the recessive trait. Therefore, q2
= .01, so q = 0.1. The frequency of the dominant allele is represented by “p”. If there are only two alleles for a particular gene, the frequencies of the two alleles must be equal to 1. Therefore, q + p
must be equal to 1. If q + p = 1, then p = 1 – q. In this example q = 0.1, so p = 1 − 0.1 = 0.9
