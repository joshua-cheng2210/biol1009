If the tall allele is represented as T and the short allele is represented by t, the cross is: Tt X TT. Half of the progeny will be TT and half of the progeny will be Tt. Therefore, there is 0 probability of this cross producing a short plant.

This result suggests that both parents are heterozygous for the trait. If the dominant allele of the trait is “D”, and the recessive allele is “d”, that means both parents are Dd and produce an equal mixture of progeny with the following genotypes (= allele combinations): DD, Dd, dD and dd. The first three genotypes will produce the dominant phenotype (observable trait) and the fourth will produce the recessive phenotype. Therefore, the offspring will have a 3:1 ratio for the trait. If one of the parents was homozygous, you would see a different ratio of traits among the offspring. For example, if one parent was DD and the other was Dd, the offspring would be an equal mix of DD and Dd. In that case, all the progeny would exhibit the dominant trait. If one parent was dd and the other was Dd, half the progeny would be dd and the other half would be Dd. In that case, ½ the progeny would exhibit the dominant trait and the other ½ would exhibit the recessive trait.

The parents must both be carriers of PKU . Tom, who is not affected, has a 1/3 chance of not being a carrier and a 2/3 chance of being a carrier. To figure this out, realize that the fact that Tom’s parents must both be carriers means they are both heterozygous for the disease-causing allele. If we represent the disease causing allele of this gene as “d”, and the non-disease causing allele of this gene as “D”, that means Tom’s parents are both Dd. Therefore, each of the children of Tom’s parents would have an equal chance of having inherited one of the following: DD, Dd, dD or dd. Because Tom does not exhibit the disease, we can rule out dd. That means he is DD, Dd or dD. If he is Dd or dD, he is a carrier of the disease. If he is DD, he is not a carrier of the disease. Therefore there is a 2/3 chance that he is a carrier (i.e. has a “d” allele).
