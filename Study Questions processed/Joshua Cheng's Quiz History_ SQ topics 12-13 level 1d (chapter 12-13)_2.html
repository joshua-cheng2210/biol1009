During meiosis I, pairs of homologous chromosomes line up together in the center of the cell, at the metaphase plate. How each pair of chromosomes orient is random, meaning that the paternal chromosome of one pair might be nearer the top of the cell while the maternal chromosome of a different pair might be nearer the top of the cell. As a result, the maternal and paternal chromosomes of each pair assort independently into the daughter cells. This is known as “independent assortment of chromosomes”.

Although chromosomes do need to form more compact structures to fit within nuclei, the coiling that occurs during mitosis condenses the chromosomes beyond what is need for the chromosomes to fit within the nucleus. Centromeres are not formed by chromosome coiling and chromosome coiling is not what holds sister chromatids together. Instead, the coiling that occurs during mitosis is necessary to allow the chromosomes to align in the middle of the cell and then to move apart during mitosis.

During anaphase the cohesin proteins holding each pair of sister chromatids together are cleaved, allowing the sister chromatids to separate and become independent chromosomes. If there are 20 centromeres in a cell during anaphase, that means that there were 10 pairs of sister chromatids, each of which has separated to form 20 chromosomes, each with its own centromere. By cloning the plant the gardener will obtain plants that are genetically identical to the original plant, and therefore share the desirable traits of the original plant. Forcing the plant to self-pollinate won’t result in a genetically identical plant. For example, if the plant was heterozygous for a particular gene (i.e. if the original plant has different alleles of a particular gene), causing the plant to self-pollinate will result in a mixture of progeny plants, where some of the progeny plants are homozygous for one of the two alleles, some progeny plants are homozygous for the other allele and some progeny plants are heterozygous (i.e. have one copy of each of the two alleles of the gene). So, self-pollination will not result in plants that are genetically identical to the original plant and can therefore be counted on to have the same desirable traits as the original plant. Similarly, breeding the plant to a plant with weaker traits will likely result in progeny plants that are not as desirable as the original plant. Back-tracking to create another similar plant is too much work and is unlikely to result in a plant that is completely identical to the original plant.

Somatic cells are all the cells in an organism, except for the gametes and the cells that give rise to the gametes. The amount of DNA per somatic cells doubles during S phase, as the cells replicate their DNA. The amount of DNA remains at this level throughout G2 phase, and then is cut in half part way through mitosis as the cell divides and each of the resulting daughter cells receives half of the DNA. So, somatic cells in G1 and G0 (remember that G0 is the phase that G1 cells enter if they don’t pass the checkpoint at the end of G1 that would allow them to enter the S phase) have half as much DNA as somatic cells during G2 and the first part of mitosis. During meiosis, two rounds of cell division occur with only one round of DNA synthesis. As a result, the gametes formed by meiosis have only half as much DNA as a somatic cell during the G1 or G0 phases, and only a quarter as much DNA as a somatic cell during G2 or the earlier part of mitosis. Therefore, sample I represents somatic cells in G1, G0 or the last part of mitosis. Sample II represents somatic cells in G2 or the earlier part of mitosis. Sample III represents gametes.
