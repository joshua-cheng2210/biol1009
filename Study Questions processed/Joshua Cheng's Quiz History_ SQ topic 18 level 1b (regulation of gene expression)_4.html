The genes in the tryptophan (trp) operon encode enzymes that are used to make tryptophan.  So, cells only want to express these genes when the cells are low in tryptophan.  This means that cells turn off expression of the genes of the trp operon whenever substantial amounts of tryptophan are present in the cell.  The mechanism by which this occurs is that tryptophan itself is a corepressor for the repressor protein for the trp operon.  When there are lots of tryptophan molecules in a cell, the tryptophan molecules will interact with the repressor protein for the trp operon, causing the repressor protein to be stabilized in its active form.  The active repressor/corepressor complex will then bind the operator nucleotide sequence for the trp
operon, blocking transcription of the gene contained within the trp
operon by RNA polymerase.

When a repressor protein is in its active shape, the active repressor protein will bind its operator blocking transcription of the genes in that operon by RNA polymerase.  When a repressor protein is in its inactive shape, it will be unable to bind its operator sequence, and RNA polymerase will be able to transcribe the genes in that operon.  Tryptophan is the corepressor for the trp repressor protein, meaning that it stabilizes trp repressor in its active form and so helps trp repressor do its job of repressing transcription of the genes in the trp operon.  This makes sense if you remember that the genes of the trp operon encode polypeptides needed for synthesis of tryptophan.  If a cell already has a lot of tryptophan, why would it want to make proteins whose job it is to make more tryptophan?  The answer is that cells do not want to waste resources on making polypeptides for tryptophan biosynthesis if they already have plently of tryptophan.  Consequently the system is set up so that tryptophan stabilizes the trp repressor protein in its active shape, so that the trp repressor protein is able to bind the trp operator and block transcription of the trp genes by RNA polymerase.

Finally, remember that "inducers" and "co-repressors" are NOT named based on how they affect the repressor proteins with which they interact.  Instead, they are named based on how they affect transcription of the genes in the operon they help regulate.  So, "inducers" help increase transcription of the genes in the operon they regulate by stabilizing the repressor protein for that operon in its inactive shape.  In contrast, "co-repressors" help decrease transcription of the genes in the operon they regulate by stabilizing the repressor protein for that operon in its active shape.

When trying to remember the difference between inducers and corepressors of repressor proteins, remember that these small molecules are NOT named because of how they affect the activity of the repressor protein.  Instead, they are named because of how they affect the rate of transcription of the genes in the operon(s) they help regulate.  So, inducers are called inducers because scientists used to think that they induce (i.e. turn on) expression of the genes in the operons they help regulate. We now know that what inducers actually do is to stabilize repressor proteins in their inactive form.  By stabilizing repressor proteins in their inactive form, inducers keep the repressor proteins from binding to the operator sequences and preventing transcription.  So, in a sense, inducers allow gene transcription to proceed by turning off a repressor.   Corepressors work in kind of the opposite way.  Corepressors stabilize the repressor proteins with which they interact in their active form.  So, when the correct corepressor is present in a cell, the repressor protein with which it interacts will bind to it and be stabilized in its active form.  The active repressor/corepressor complex will then bind the operator sequence, preventing transcription of the genes in the operon by RNA polymerase.  So, the name corepressor refers to the fact that corepressors help repress transcription of the genes in the operon.
