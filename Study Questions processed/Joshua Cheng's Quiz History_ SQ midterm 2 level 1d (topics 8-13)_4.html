If a cell is carrying out glycolysis in the absence of sufficient oxygen, the NADH formed during glycolysis must be “re-cycled” back to NAD+
so that glycolysis can continue to occur.  In cells carrying out anaerobic respiration, recycling of NADH to NAD+ occurs by an electron transport chain that uses a final electron acceptor that is something other than oxygen (e.g. sulfate).  In cells carrying out fermentation, the fermentation reaction results in conversion of the NADH formed during glycolysis back to NAD+, so that the reactions of glycolysis can continue to occur.  So, there is no net production of NADH in cells carrying out fermentation.  Similarly, the pyruvate formed by glycolysis is converted to some other form (typically ethanol or lactate) during fermentation.  Whether a cell produces ethanol or lactate or something else via fermentation depends on the cell.  Yeast cells undergoing fermentation form ethanol.  Muscle cells undergoing fermentation produce lactate (also known as lactic acid).

The substance that is “gaining” an electron, either through the complete transfer of an electron from one atom to another or through a shift in the sharing of a pair of electrons in a covalent bond, has its charge reduced and so is said to have been reduced.  In contrast, the substance that is “losing” an electron will have its charge increased (because electrons are negatively charged), and so is said to have been oxidized.  In this example, sodium (Na) transferred an electron to chlorine (Cl), so the sodium atom was oxidized (its charge increased because it lost a negatively charged electron) and the chlorine atom was reduced (its charge was reduced because it gained a negatively charged electron).

At the “M” (for metaphase) checkpoint the cell checks to make sure that kinetochore microtubules are attached to the kinetochore of each of its chromatids. Enzymes do not alter the free energy change of a reaction and so do not alter the point at which equilibrium will be reached or the overall direction of the reaction.  In contrast, the direction of a reversible reaction will be determined by the relative concentrations of the reactants and products of the reactions, together with the difference in the free energies of the reactant and product molecules.

During photosynthesis, "resonance energy transfer" refers to the transfer of energy from an excited electron in one pigment molecule to an electron in a different pigment molecule.  Remember that the pigment molecules in the light harvesting complexes transfer energy from an excited electron between pigment molecules, and finally to special chlorophyll a molecules in the reaction center, via resonance energy transfer.  Only the energy from the excited electron is transferred during this process, not the actual electron.  In contrast, special chlorophyll a molecules in the reaction center complexes transfer the actual excited electron to a primary electron acceptor.

At equilibrium, the free energy change is zero and no work can be done.  So, a cell where most reactions are at equilibrium would not be able to do enough work and so would be dead.  Also, the "products" of reversible reactions are frequently converted to other substances or transported to other cellular compartments before they can be metabolized via the reverse reaction, which also prevents many cellular reactions from reaching equilibrium.
