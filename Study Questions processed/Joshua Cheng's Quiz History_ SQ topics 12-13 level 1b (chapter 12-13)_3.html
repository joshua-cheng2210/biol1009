The amount of DNA per chromosome is not affected by tissue type. However, the amount of DNA per chromosome will be twice as high in cells where the DNA has replicated but the resulting sister chromatids are still together (i.e. part of the same chromosome) than in cells where sister chromatids have separated. For example, in G1 phase the DNA has not yet replicated. In contrast, G2
phase occurs after DNA synthesis but before mitosis. So, during G2
phase each chromosome consists of two sister chromatids that are stuck together at the centromere. Therefore, chromosomes in cells in G2 phase have twice as much DNA as chromosomes in cells in G1 phase. Prior to telophase the sister chromatids separate, becoming independent chromosomes. Consequently the amount of DNA per chromosome for cells in telophase is the same as for cells in G1, but half as much as for cells in G2.

Spindle microtubules originate from centrosomes. Some of the microtubules originating from the centrosomes bind to kinetochores. Kinetochores are specialized protein complexes located at the centromeres. The kinetochores of sister chromatids are attached to microtubules originating from different centrosomes. This becomes important during anaphase, when sister chromatids separate and move apart as they move towards centrosomes on opposite sides of the cell.

You can distinguish between the different phases of the cell cycle based on the amount of DNA per nucleus, as the amount of DNA per nucleus changes over the course of a cell cycle. Nuclei have the lowest amount of DNA during G1, which is represented by I and V in the figure. During the next phase of the cell cycle, the S phase (II in the figure), DNA synthesis occurs and so the amount of DNA per nucleus doubles during S phase. The amount of DNA per nucleus does not change during the next phase of the cell cycle, the G2
phase (III in the figure). During mitosis, the nucleus divides with each of the resulting daughter nuclei receiving half of the DNA. So, during mitosis (IV in the figure), the amount of DNA per nuclei decreases to half of what it was during G2.

Alleles are different versions of the same gene that differ slightly in their DNA sequences. A diploid organism has two sets of chromosomes, meaning that it has two copies of each gene.
