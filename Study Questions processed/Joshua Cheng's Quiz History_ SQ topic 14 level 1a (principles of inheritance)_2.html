The parents must both be carriers of PKU . Tom, who is not affected, has a 1/3 chance of not being a carrier and a 2/3 chance of being a carrier. To figure this out, realize that the fact that Tom’s parents must both be carriers means they are both heterozygous for the disease-causing allele. If we represent the disease causing allele of this gene as “d”, and the non-disease causing allele of this gene as “D”, that means Tom’s parents are both Dd. Therefore, each of the children of Tom’s parents would have an equal chance of having inherited one of the following: DD, Dd, dD or dd. Because Tom does not exhibit the disease, we can rule out dd. That means he is DD, Dd or dD. If he is Dd or dD, he is a carrier of the disease. If he is DD, he is not a carrier of the disease. Therefore there is a 2/3 chance that he is a carrier (i.e. has a “d” allele).

Radishes that are heterozygous for this flower color gene display a phenotype that is intermediate to the phenotype of radishes that are homozygous for either allele. Such an intermediate phenotype is characteristic of alleles that exhibit incomplete dominance. In contrast, “codominance” is when the traits of both alleles are expressed. For example, cows that are heterozygous for red and white hair color alleles produce some hairs that are red and some that are white. So, they express both the red and white phenotypes. If the cow hair color alleles were instead incompletely dominant, cows that were heterozygous for the hair color gene would make all pink hairs.

The fact that a cross between radishes with red and white flowers yields all-purple offspring tells us that the red and white alleles of the flower color gene must exhibit incomplete dominance, so that a red/white heterozygote has a phenotype intermediate to that of red/red or white/white homozygotes. Therefore, all the F1 progeny from a cross between a plant that is true breeding (homozygous) for the red allele and a plant that is homozygous for the white allele will be heterozygous for the flower color gene, and therefore purple. In contrast, the long allele of the radish shape gene is dominant over the oval allele. Therefore, a cross between a plant that is homozygous for the long allele and a plant that is homozygous for the oval allele will result in all F1 progeny being heterozygous for the radish shape gene, and thus having long radishes. Therefore, all the F1
progeny will have long, purple radishes.
