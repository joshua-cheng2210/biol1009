Normally operator sequences lie between promoter sequences (where RNA polymerase binds) and the structural genes of the operon.  This arrangement means that, if active repressor protein is bound to the operator sequence, it will physically block the movement of RNA polymerase along the mRNA molecule towards the structural genes of the operon.  So, the normal placement of the operator sequence between the promoter sequence and the structural genes of the operon is necessary for its function.  If you move the operator sequence so that it is on the far side of the the promoter from the structural genes, even if active repressor protein binds the operator sequence, it wont be in the right place to be able to block transcription of the structural genes.
 
Normal situation:
Promoter sequences (where RNA polymerase binds)----operator sequences (where active repressor protein binds)---structural genes
 
Situation after research moves the lac operator sequences:
 
Operator sequences (where active repressor protein binds)----promoter sequences (where RNA polymerase binds)----structural genes.
 
See how, in the artificial case, even if active repressor protein is bound to the operator sequences it wont be between RNA polymerase and the genes to be transcribed and therefore wont be able to prevent RNA polymerase from being able to transcribe the genes all of the time?
