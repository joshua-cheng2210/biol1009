According to the biological species concept, a species is a group of populations whose members have the potential to interbreed in nature and produce viable, fertile offspring, but that do not produce viable, fertile offspring with members of other such groups. So, by defining species on the basis of being able to interbreed and produce viable, fertile offspring, the biological species concept focuses on genetic exchange (via interbreeding).

Although speciation often occurs very slowly and involves a large number of genetic changes, it can occur very quickly and involve only a single genetic change. Although speciation often occurs in isolated populations, it can also occur in populations that are not geographically isolated from each other. For example, mistakes in cell division can lead to an organism being polyploid (having more than 2 sets of chromosomes). Polyploid organisms are not able to produce fertile offspring when crossed with non-polyploid (e.g. diploid) members of the species from which they evolved.

Think of it this way: If you were asked to build a polymer that was just one monomer long (ok, that wouldnt be a polymer, but lets ignore that for the moment), and you had only A and B monomers available to use, you would be able to build just two different polymers: A and B.  If you were asked to build a polymer that was just 2 monomers long (again, that wouldnt be a polymer, but lets ignore that for the moment), and had only A and B monomers available, you would be able to build just 4 different polymers: AA, AB, BA and BB. If you were asked to build a polymer that was just 3 monomers long (again, that wouldnt be a polymer, but lets ignore that for the moment), and had only A and B monomers available, you would be able to build just 8 different polymers: AAA, AAB, ABA, BAA, ABB, BAB. BBA and BBB.  Are you seeing a pattern here?  The answer to this type of question may be found using the following formula: Number of different polymers that may be formed = XY, where X is the number of different types of monomers available and Y is the number of monomers in the polymer.  So, if you were asked to build a polymer that is 20 monomers long, and you could use four different types of monomers, the number of different polymers you could build would be: 420 = ~ 1 x 1012
