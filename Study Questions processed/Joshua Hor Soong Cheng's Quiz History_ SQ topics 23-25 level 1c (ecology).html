Good work!

The logistic model of population growth assumes there is a maximum population size that the environment can support and that population growth slows as the population approaches that carrying capacity. In this model, the change in population size (dN) over time (dt) is represented by rN x (K N)/ K. In this equation, r represents the rate of population growth if growth rates are unlimited by population density, and N represents the number of individuals in the population. So, if growth rates were unrestricted (i.e. if growth rates were exponential), dN/dt
would be equal to rN. However, in this example the population growth is not un-limited, it is increasingly restricted as the size of the population nears the carrying capacity (maximum number of individuals that can be supported) of the environment. In other words, the population experiences logistic growth. The carrying capacity of the environment is symbolized by K. The effects on growth rate of the actual size of the population in relation to the carrying capacity are calculated by (K N)/K. If the size of N is very small in comparison to K (i.e. if the area can support many, many more individuals than are currently present), then (K N)/K will be approximately equal to K/K, or 1, and the rate of population growth will be approximately equal to rN. If the size of N is approximately the same as K (i.e. if the number of individuals already present is close to the carrying capacity), then (K N)/K will be approximately equal to zero, and their will be little to no population growth.

Good job!

Normal, clean water is actually somewhat acidic, as it has a pH typically between 5.0 and 5.5, which is below the pH of pure water, which is 7.0. Acid rain is more acidic then normal, clean rain, and has a pH typically between 4.0 and 4.5, although readings as low as 3.0 have been found. Remember that pH is equal to the negative log of the hydrogen ion concentration. So, a lower pH value corresponds with a higher concentration of hydrogen ions.

A producer is an organism that produces organic compounds from CO2 by harnessing light energy (photosynthesis) or by oxidizing inorganic chemicals (in chemosynthetic reactions carried out by some prokaryotes). A primary consumer is an herbivore; an organism that eats plants or other animals. A secondary consumer is a carnivore that eats herbivores. A tertiary consumer is a carnivore that eats other carnivores (i.e. secondary consumers). A food chain begins with a producer. Next comes a primary consumer (e.g. a grasshopper), then a secondary consumer (e.g. a lizard) and then a tertiary consumer (e.g. a hawk).
