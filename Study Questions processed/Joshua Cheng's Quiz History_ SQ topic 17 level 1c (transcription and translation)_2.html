The template strand of a DNA molecule is the strand that is hydrogen bonded to complementary ribonucleotides during the formation of an RNA molecule. So, the sequence of the RNA molecule will be complementary to the sequence of the DNA template strand.  Also, remember that RNA molecules contain U in place of T.  So, if the template strand is GGG, the mRNA will have the sequence CCC.  The sequence of the anticodon that binds to CCC on the mRNA will thus be GGG.

Remember that RNA polymerase, like DNA polymerase, adds nucleotides to the 3’ end of the growing nucleotide chain.  Also remember that RNA polymerase uses the DNA template strand as its template, meaning that the ribonucleotides of the RNA molecule will be hydrogen bonded with the deoxyribonucleotides of the template strand.  Finally, remember that the template strand and the RNA molecule will be hydrogen bonded in an antiparallel arrangement, meaning that the 5’ end of the template strand will be hydrogen bonded with the 3’ end of the RNA molecule and the 3’ end of the template strand be hydrogen bonded with the 5’ end of the RNA molecule.  Putting this all together, RNA polymerase will begin synthesis of a new RNA molecule by adding ribonucleotides that are hydrogen bonded with the 3’ end of the template strand, as these ribonucleotides will thus form the 5’ end of the RNA strand.  So, the promoter (the DNA sequence where the RNA polymerase binds) must be to the right of the template strand.

The coding segments of a stretch of eukaryotic DNA are known as exons.  The DNA sequences that lie between the exons of a gene are known as “intervening” sequences, or “introns”.  Introns are excised (removed, or “spliced out”) during processing of pre-mRNAs, so that mature mRNAs do not contain introns.  Note that the DNA sequences that code for the 5’ and 3’ untranslated regions of mRNAs are considered to be parts of exons, even though they are not translated, because they do still code for part of a mature mRNA molecule.

The WORST mutations that were listed are the insertion of 1, 2 or 4 nucleotides.  The reason for this is that such mutations will cause what is known as a “frame shift”.  A frame shift mutation causes all the codons (“words”) to be read incorrectly.  For example, if a particular DNA sequence has codons as follows:  ATG AGG CCC TTT, adding two nucleotides (e.g. adding two G nucleotides to the beginning of the molecule) would change the codons to:  GGA TGA GGC CCT etc.  These frame shifted codons would specify different amino acids than the non frame shifted codons, so that the amino acid sequence of the polypeptide made from the mutated gene would be almost completely different than the amino acid sequence of the polypeptide made from a gene that is not mutated.  Inserting 1 or 4 nucleotides will also cause a frame shift mutation.  In contrast, inserting 3 nucleotides will not cause a frame shift, and so the mutated gene will produce a polypeptide that is almost identical in sequence to the polypeptide produced by the unmutated gene.  The polypeptide produced by this mutated gene will just have one extra amino acid and possibly, depending on exactly where the 3 nucleotides are inserted, one amino acid that is switched to a different amino acid.  Such a small change in amino acid sequence may have only a small affect on the folding of the polypeptide into the correct 3-dimensional shape, and the polypeptide may still be able to carry out most or all of its normal functions.

When the ribosome reaches a stop codon on the mRNA, the stop codon will be aligned with the A (aminoacyl) site of the ribosome.  Normally, a tRNA with an appropriate anticodon binds the mRNA codon that is aligned with the A site of the ribosome.  However, when the codon that is aligned with the A site is a stop codon, a protein called a “release factor” will bind the stop codon in the A site instead of a tRNA. When this happens, the polypeptide chain attached to the tRNA in the ribosome P site is cleaved from the tRNA and the translation assembly (e.g. the ribosome, tRNA and mRNA) comes apart.
