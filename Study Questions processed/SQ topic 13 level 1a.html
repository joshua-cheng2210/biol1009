Chromosome replication occurs prior to meiosis I and prior to mitosis. Meiosis I, meiosis II and mitosis all result in the production of daughter cells. Chromosome condensation needs to occur during both mitosis and meiosis, to allow chromosomes to move around the cell without becoming tangled and breaking. In contrast, synapsis only occurs during meiosis I. Remember that synapsis is when homologous chromosomes become stuck together by proteins of the synaptonemal complex during prophase I.

Nice work!

By cloning the plant the gardener will obtain plants that are genetically identical to the original plant, and therefore share the desirable traits of the original plant. Forcing the plant to self-pollinate wont result in a genetically identical plant. For example, if the plant was heterozygous for a particular gene (i.e. if the original plant has different alleles of a particular gene), causing the plant to self-pollinate will result in a mixture of progeny plants, where some of the progeny plants are homozygous for one of the two alleles, some progeny plants are homozygous for the other allele and some progeny plants are heterozygous (i.e. have one copy of each of the two alleles of the gene). So, self-pollination will not result in plants that are genetically identical to the original plant and can therefore be counted on to have the same desirable traits as the original plant. Similarly, breeding the plant to a plant with weaker traits will likely result in progeny plants that are not as desirable as the original plant. Back-tracking to create another similar plant is too much work and is unlikely to result in a plant that is completely identical to the original plant.

During meiosis I, one of each pair of homologous chromosomes ends up in each daughter cells. So, at the completion of meiosis I the resulting daughter cells have half as much DNA and half as many chromosomes as a cell that has replicated its DNA and is about to begin meiosis. At the completion of meiosis II, the pair of sister chromatids making up each chromosome separate, with each daughter cell receiving one of the chromatids, which are each now independent chromosomes. As a result, the number of chromosomes per cell does not decrease during meiosis II, but the amount of DNA per cell is again cut in half. Therefore, the overall process of meiosis I and II results in a 4-fold reduction in the amount of DNA per cell and a 2-fold reduction in the number of chromosomes per cell.

A diploid organism has two sets of chromosomes, meaning that it has two copies of each gene in its somatic cells (remember that somatic cells are all the cells in an organism, except for the gametes and the cells that produce the gametes). Slightly different versions of the same gene are known as alleles. So, for each gene, a diploid organism can have either two copies of the same allele or two different alleles of that gene. During meiosis, the pairs of homologous chromosomes separate and the daughter cells that result at the end of meiosis have only one set of chromosomes and therefore only one allele of each gene. In this example, the female in question has different alleles of the wing color gene but the same (short) allele of the antennae length gene. Therefore, half the egg cells produced by this female will have one copy of the blue allele of the wing color gene and the other half of the egg cells will have one copy of the orange allele. Because this female only has the short allele of the antennae length gene, all the egg cells produced by this female will have one copy of the short allele of the antennae length gene on chromosome 19.

During anaphase, either sister chromatids separate (anaphase of mitosis and meiosis II), or pairs of homologous chromosomes separate (anaphase of meiosis I). Removal of the cohesin proteins holding together the chromatids in each pair of sister chromatids is thus clearly necessary for anaphase of mitosis and meiosis II to occur. Removal of cohesins along the chromosome arms is also necessary for anaphase of meiosis I to occur because the crossing over between homologous chromosomes that occurs prior to anaphase I results in DNA being exchanged between pairs of homologous chromosomes. As a result, the pairs of homologous chromosomes are stuck together by the cohesin proteins along the chromosome arms and cant separate during anaphase I if the cohesin proteins are still present. For example, in the figure shown below crossing over has occurred between the blue and purple homologous chromosomes. In the figure, cohesin proteins (shown as small black circles) are present and are holding together DNA from each of the original pairs of sister chromatids. As a result, part of each chromosome is stuck to the other chromosome and the two homologous chromosomes will be unable to separate until the cohesin along the chromosome arms is degraded.

Somatic cells are all the cells in an organism, except for the gametes and the cells that give rise to the gametes. The amount of DNA per somatic cells doubles during S phase, as the cells replicate their DNA. The amount of DNA remains at this level throughout G2 phase, and then is cut in half part way through mitosis as the cell divides and each of the resulting daughter cells receives half of the DNA. So, somatic cells in G1 and G0 (remember that G0 is the phase that G1 cells enter if they dont pass the checkpoint at the end of G1 that would allow them to enter the S phase) have half as much DNA as somatic cells during G2 and the first part of mitosis. During meiosis, two rounds of cell division occur with only one round of DNA synthesis. As a result, the gametes formed by meiosis have only half as much DNA as a somatic cell during the G1 or G0 phases, and only a quarter as much DNA as a somatic cell during G2 or the earlier part of mitosis. When two gametes combine to form a zygote, the resulting zygote has twice as much DNA as either gamete, or the same amount of DNA as a somatic cell during G1 or G0. Therefore, sample I represents a newly formed zygote, or somatic cells in G1, G0 or the last part of mitosis. Sample II represents somatic cells in G2 or the earlier part of mitosis. Sample III represents gametes.

Even if crossing over does not occur, many different chromosomal combinations are possible. For example, if a species has a haploid number of 2 chromosomes and no crossing over occurs, the following 4 combinations are possible for the gametes:
homolog 1 of chromosome 1 + homolog 1 of chromosome 2;
homolog 1 of chromosome 1 + homolog 2 of chromosome 2;
homolog 2 of chromosome 1 + homolog 1 of chromosome 2;
homolog 2 of chromosome 1 + homolog 2 of chromosome 2.

 
If a species has a haploid number of 3 chromosomes, the following 8 combinations are possible:
homolog 1 of chromosome 1 + homolog 1 of chromosome 2 + homolog 1 of chromosome 3; homolog 1 of chromosome 1 + homolog 1 of chromosome 2 + homolog 2 of chromosome 3; homolog 1 of chromosome 1 + homolog 2 of chromosome 2 + homolog 1 of chromosome 3; homolog 1 of chromosome 1 + homolog 2 of chromosome 2 + homolog 2 of chromosome 3; homolog 2 of chromosome 1 + homolog 1 of chromosome 2 + homolog 1 of chromosome 3; homolog 2 of chromosome 1 + homolog 1 of chromosome 2 + homolog 2 of chromosome 3; homolog 2 of chromosome 1 + homolog 2 of chromosome 2 + homolog 1 of chromosome 3; homolog 2 of chromosome 1 + homolog 2 of chromosome 2 + homolog 2 of chromosome 3.
