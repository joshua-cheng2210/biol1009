A component of an electron transport chain will first receive an electron from the component immediately preceding it (i.e. the component that is slightly less electronegative), and will then pass that electron on to the next component in the chain (i.e. the component that is slightly more electronegative than it).  So, each component of an electron transport chain alternates between receiving and donating electrons, and thus between acting as an oxidizing agent (when it receives an electron) and a reducing agent (when it donates an electron, causing the substance to which the electron was donated to have a REDUCED charge).

Nice job!

Competitive inhibitors “compete” with substrate molecules for binding to the enzyme’s active site.  Addition of more substrate does, therefore, help reverse the effects of a competitive inhibitor by allowing the increased number of substrate molecules to occupy the active sites of more of the enzyme molecules.  Because competitive inhibitors bind at the enzyme’s active site, they are by definition NOT allosteric regulators, because the definition of an allosteric regulator is a regulator that binds at a site that is distinct from the enzyme’s active site.

Chromosome replication occurs prior to meiosis I and prior to mitosis. Meiosis I, meiosis II and mitosis all result in the production of daughter cells. Chromosome condensation needs to occur during both mitosis and meiosis, to allow chromosomes to move around the cell without becoming tangled and breaking.
