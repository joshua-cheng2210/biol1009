Normally operator sequences lie between promoter sequences (where RNA polymerase binds) and the structural genes of the operon.  This arrangement means that, if active repressor protein is bound to the operator sequence, it will physically block the movement of RNA polymerase along the mRNA molecule towards the structural genes of the operon.  So, the normal placement of the operator sequence between the promoter sequence and the structural genes of the operon is necessary for its function.  If you move the operator sequence so that it is on the far side of the structural genes from the promoter, even if active repressor protein binds the operator sequence, it wont be in the right place to be able to block transcription of the structural genes.

The degree of DNA methylation and the presence of certain transcription factors will affect the rate at which the DNA molecule is transcribed to form RNA, but will not affect the rate at which the mRNA made from that molecule is translated to form protein. Similarly, the number of introns may affect the rate at which the primary transcript is processed to form a mature mRNA, but since the introns will no longer be present in the mature mRNA will have no effect on the rate at which the mRNA molecule is translated to form protein. In contrast, the rate at which the mRNA molecule is degraded will affect the amount of protein made by that mRNA molecule. If it is degraded very quickly, it may have been translated only once or twice. If it is degraded very slowly, it may have had time to have been translated many times before being degraded.

The genes in the tryptophan (trp) operon encode enzymes that are used to make tryptophan.  So, cells only want to express these genes when the cells are low in tryptophan.  This means that cells turn off expression of the genes of the trp operon whenever substantial amounts of tryptophan are present in the cell.  The mechanism by which this occurs is that tryptophan itself is a corepressor for the repressor protein for the trp operon.  When there are lots of tryptophan molecules in a cell, the tryptophan molecules will interact with the repressor protein for the trp operon, causing the repressor protein to be stabilized in its active form.  The active repressor/corepressor complex will then bind the operator nucleotide sequence for the trp
operon, blocking transcription of the gene contained within the trp
operon by RNA polymerase.

Remember that RNA polymerase, like DNA polymerase, adds nucleotides to the 3 end of the growing nucleotide chain. Also remember that RNA polymerase uses the DNA template strand as its template, meaning that the ribonucleotides of the RNA molecule will be hydrogen bonded with the deoxyribonucleotides of the template strand. Finally, remember that the template strand and the RNA molecule will be hydrogen bonded in an antiparallel arrangement, meaning that the 5 end of the template strand will be hydrogen bonded with the 3 end of the RNA molecule and the 3 end of the template strand be hydrogen bonded with the 5 end of the RNA molecule. Putting this all together, RNA polymerase will begin synthesis of a new RNA molecule by adding ribonucleotides that are hydrogen bonded with the 3 end of the template strand, as these ribonucleotides will thus form the 5 end of the RNA strand. The RNA polymerase will then move in the 3 to 5 direction along the template strand, so that it can add ribonucleotides to the growing RNA molecule in the 5 to 3 direction.
