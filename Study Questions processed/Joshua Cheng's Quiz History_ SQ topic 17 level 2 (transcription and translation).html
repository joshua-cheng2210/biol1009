Nice job!

The two DNA strands that form a gene are known as the coding and template strands. The template strand is the strand to which ribonucleotides hydrogen bond during the formation of an RNA molecule. So, the RNA molecule will be the complement of the template strand.  The coding strand is also the complement of the template strand.  Therefore, the mRNA molecule will have the same sequence as the coding strand of the DNA, with the exception that T will be replaced with U in the RNA molecule.  The anticodon that binds an RNA molecule will be the complement of that molecule. 
 
What does this all mean?  If the coding sequence is GGG, the sequence of the mRNA will also be GGG.  The sequence of the anticodon that binds this codon on the mRNA will therefore be CCC.

When a new, charged tRNA enters the ribosome, it enters at the A (amino acyl) site of the ribosome. At that point in time, the tRNA to which the growing polypeptide is attached is in the P (peptidyl tRNA) site of the ribosome. After the new, charged tRNA enters the A site of the ribosome, the ribosome catalyzes the breaking of the covalent bond connecting the polypeptide with the tRNA in the P site and then catalyzes to formation of a new covalent bond between the polypeptide and the amino acid attached to the tRNA in the A site. After that happens, the ribosome shifts position along the mRNA molecule such that the tRNA that was in the P site is now in the E site, and the tRNA that was in the A site is now in the P site. The tRNA that is now in the E (exit) site now leaves the ribosome. A new, charged tRNA enters the A site, and the whole process is repeated.

When we say that the genetic code is almost universal, what we mean is that almost all organisms use the same codons to specify the same amino acids. In other words, ATG will specify the amino acid methionine in almost every organism. Because of this, a gene that is transferred from one species to another will almost always code for a protein of the same amino acid sequence in the new species as in the original species from which the gene came. When we say that the genetic code is redundant, what we mean is that most amino acids are specified by more than one codon. For example, there are six different codons that all specify the amino acid leucine. When we say that the genetic code is non-ambiguous, what we mean is that each codon specifies only one amino acid. So, multiple codons can specify the same amino acid, but a given codon only specifies a single amino acid. When we say that the genetic code exhibits wobble, what we mean is that the anticodons of some tRNAs are able to base pair with multiple codons that have almost identical sequences. In cases where a tRNA can base pair with multiple codons, each of the codons the tRNA can base pair with will specify the same amino acid. So, wobble does NOT cause the genetic code to be ambiguous. A particular tRNA will always help insert the same amino acid into a polypeptide, its just that the tRNA may act at more than one codon that specifies that particular amino acid.

Nice work!

Normally, when the ribosome reaches a stop codon on the mRNA, the stop codon will be aligned with the A (aminoacyl) site of the ribosome. A protein called a release factor will bind the stop codon in the A site. When this happens, the polypeptide chain attached to the tRNA in the ribosome P site is cleaved from the tRNA and the translation assembly (e.g. the ribosome, tRNA and mRNA) comes apart. In this experiment, this process was arrested, meaning that no release factor was allowed to enter the A site. As a consequence, the polypeptide will still be attached to the tRNA in the P site and the ribosome will not have dissociated from the mRNA molecule.
