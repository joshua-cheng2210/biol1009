In general, a bacterial operon will be transcribed by RNA polymerase unless an active repressor protein is able to bind to the operator sequence, blocking transcription of the genes in the operon by RNA polymerase.  If the mutation inactivates the function of the repressor protein, the repressor protein will never be able to bind the operator sequence and block transcription.  Consequently, the genes of the operon will always be transcribed.

In eukaryotes, precursor mRNAs, or pre-mRNAs (also known as primary RNAs), can code for more than one slightly different polypeptides as a result of alternative splicing of the pre-mRNAs to form slightly different mature mRNAs.  However, mature eukaryotic mRNAs only code for a single polypeptide.  In contrast, mature prokaryotic mRNAs often code for multiple, completely different, polypeptides.

When a new, charged tRNA enters the ribosome, it enters at the A (amino acyl) site of the ribosome. At that point in time, the tRNA to which the growing polypeptide is attached is in the P (peptidyl tRNA) site of the ribosome. In this example, the tRNA to which the growing polypeptide is attached is tRNA #2. After the new, charged tRNA (tRNA #1 in this question) enters the A site of the ribosome, the ribosome catalyzes the breaking of the covalent bond connecting the polypeptide with the tRNA in the P site (= tRNA #2) and then catalyzes to formation of a new covalent bond between the polypeptide and the amino acid attached to the tRNA in the A site (lysine in this example). After that happens, the ribosome shifts position along the mRNA molecule such that the tRNA that was in the P site (tRNA #2) is now in the E site, and the tRNA that was in the A site (tRNA #1, which now has the growing polypeptide chain attached to it) is now in the P site. The tRNA (tRNA #2) which is now in the E (exit) site now leaves the ribosome. A new, charged tRNA enters the A site, and the whole process is repeated.
