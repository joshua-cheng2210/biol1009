For a reaction to be a spontaneous reaction, the free energy of the reactant molecules must be greater than the free energy of the product molecules, so that free energy decreases as a result of the reaction. So, if you imagine a reaction where A is converted to B, the conversion of a molecule of A to a molecule of B will be spontaneous if the free energy of molecule A is greater than the free energy of molecule B. So, if on average A molecules have a free energy of 5 kcal/mole and B molecules have a free energy of 4.9 kcal/mole, the average free energy of a molecule of A will be greater than the average free energy of a molecule of B and therefore the conversion of an average molecule of A to an average molecule of B will represent an exergonic, or spontaneous, reaction. However, what we have to realize is that different molecules of A in a solution will have slightly different free energies and different molecules of B will have slightly free different energies. So, if the AVERAGE free energy of a molecule of B is 4.9 kcal/mole, there may be some molecules of B that have a free energy of greater than 5 kcal/mole. Those molecules of B that have a free energy that is greater than 5 kcal/mole can then be converted to molecules of A via a reaction that will be exergonic (spontaneous). Note that this does NOT mean that the conversion of B to A will occur at as high a rate as the conversion of A to B. If there are only a very small number of molecules of B that have a high enough free energy level to be converted to A via an exergonic reaction, the rate of conversion of A to B will be higher than the rate of conversion of B to A. Eventually, the high rate of conversion of A to B will result in there being enough molecules of B with sufficient free energy to be converted to A via an exergonic reaction that the rate of conversion of B to A will equal the rate of conversion of A to B. At that point, the reaction will have reached equilibrium and both reactions will occur at the same rate (note that, in this example, at equilibrium there will be more molecules of B than of A, but a smaller percentage of the molecules of B will have sufficient free energy to be converted to A via an exergonic reaction). In addition, there is a tendency for reactions to be reversible because there is greater entropy in a mixture of substances than in a pure substance. In other words, a system that contains a mixture of "A" and "B" molecules will have greater entropy than a system that has just "A" or just "B" molecules.

By definition, exergonic reactions are reactions where the reaction results in a decrease in free energy. In contrast, it is possible for an exergonic reaction to result in an increase in the TOTAL energy of the system. For that to occur, the increase in the total energy of the system must be more than offset by an increase in entropy. Because exergonic reactions are reactions that result in a decrease in free energy, there is no need for an energy input in order for an exergonic reaction to occur.

Remember that redox reaction is short for reduction-oxidation reaction.  Redox reactions are reactions where an electron is transferred between atoms or molecules, or where the sharing of electrons in covalent bonds is altered.  In the example shown above, NAD is partially gaining an electron when it is converted to NADH, because the covalent bond between the NAD and the H will be a polar covalent bond.  Because NAD is more electronegative than hydrogen, the pair of electrons that will form the covalent bond between NAD and hydrogen will spend more of their time near NAD.  So, the NAD in NADH will carry a partial negative charge and so will have been reduced during the reaction carried out in step C.  Conversely, glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate will have been oxidized as it is converted to 1,3 bisphosphoglycerate.

You can distinguish between the different phases of the cell cycle based on the amount of DNA per nucleus, as the amount of DNA per nucleus changes over the course of a cell cycle. Nuclei have the lowest amount of DNA during G1, which is represented by I and V in the figure. During the next phase of the cell cycle, the S phase (II in the figure), DNA synthesis occurs and so the amount of DNA per nucleus doubles during S phase. phase (III in the figure). During mitosis, the nucleus divides with each of the resulting daughter nuclei receiving half of the DNA. So, during mitosis (IV in the figure), the amount of DNA per nuclei decreases to half of what it was during G2.

Autosomes are chromosomes that are not sex chromosomes (e.g. not X or Y chromosomes). Somatic cells are all the cells in an organism except for the gametes (egg and sperm cells) and the cells that produce the gametes. In a diploid organism, such as a human, somatic cells have two sets of chromosomes and gametes have only one set. Human somatic cells have 22 pairs of autosomes and one pair of sex chromosomes. Gametes have only one set of autosomes and one sex chromosome. Sperm cells can have either an X or a Y chromosome for their sex chromosome. In contrast, all egg cells have an X chromosome. A zygote, which results from fertilization of an egg cell by a sperm cell, has 22 pairs of autosomes (so 44 autosomes) and two sex chromosomes, as do somatic cells of a male.

Pairs of homologous chromosomes are chromosomes where one chromosome of each pair is inherited from the mother and the other is inherited from the father. The chromosomes in a pair of homologous chromosomes are almost identical. They contain the same genes, but have slightly different versions (alleles) of most genes. As a result, each chromosome from a pair of homologous chromosomes will have the same length and centromere position. Because they have the same genes, they will also have in common the traits coded for by those genes. However, because they will have different alleles of most of their genes, they will not have identical DNA sequences.
