There is really no such thing as "oxidative phosphorylation of ATP".  Instead, oxidative phosphorylation is used to GENERATE ATP, not to phosphorylate it.

Glycolysis can occur as long as the molecules needed to carry out glycolysis, namely the enzymes that catalyze the reactions of glycolysis, food molecules (e.g. glucose), ADP and NAD+ are present. Of these, the one that tends to be where difficulties may arise is an adequate supply of NAD+. In the absence of oxygen, NAD+ may be regenerated (made) from NADH via fermentation.

The substance that is “gaining” an electron, either through the complete transfer of an electron from one atom to another or through a shift in the sharing of a pair of electrons in a covalent bond, has its charge reduced and so is said to have been “reduced”.  In contrast, the substance that is (partially or entirely) “losing” an electron will have its charge increased (because electrons are negatively charged), and so is said to have been oxidized.  In this example, the oxygen atoms in O2 are neutrally charged (i.e. they are uncharged).  The oxygen atoms in H2O have a partial negative charge because oxygen is more electronegative than hydrogen, so the electrons that form a covalent bond between an oxygen atom and a hydrogen atom will spend more of their time closer to the oxygen atom, giving the oxygen atom in a water molecule a partial negative charge.  So, in going from O2, to H2O, the oxygen atoms go from uncharged to having a partial negative charge, so their charge is reduced.  In the case of the CH4 (i.e. methane) molecule, the carbon atom in this molecule forms covalent bonds with hydrogen atoms.  The bonds between the carbon atom and the hydrogen atoms are usually considered to be nonpolar, because carbon is only slightly more electronegative than hydrogen, but the fact that carbon is a bit more electronegative than hydrogen means that the electrons forming those bonds will actually spend just a bit more of their time closer to the carbon atom than to the hydrogen atom. So, the carbon atom in CH4 will be close to neutrally charged (i.e. uncharged) but, if anything, will carry a very slight negative charge.  In contrast, in CO2 the covalent bonds between the carbon atom and the oxygen atoms are polar, as oxygen is significantly more electronegative than carbon.  Consequently the electrons forming those bonds will spend more of their time near the oxygen atoms than near the carbon atom, leaving the carbon atom with a partial positive charge.  So, the carbon atom in CH4 goes from being close to uncharged in CH4 to carrying a partial positive charge in CO2, meaning that the carbon atom is oxidized during this reaction.

In a redox (reduction-oxidation) reaction, the substance that is partially or entirely giving up one or more electrons is known as the “reducing agent”, because the negatively charged electron that is moving all or part of the way to another atom will reduce the charge on that atom.  In contrast, the substance that is partially or entirely acquiring an electron is known as the “oxidizing agent” because the partial or entire removal of an electron from an atom will cause that atom to be oxidized (i.e. have its charged increased).  So, in the reaction shown below, the sodium (Na) atom is being oxidized, and is acting as the reducing agent for the chlorine (Cl) atom.  Conversely, the chlorine atom is being reduced and is acting as the oxidizing agent for the sodium atom.
