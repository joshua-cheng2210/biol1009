Good work!

Although chromosomes do need to form more compact structures to fit within nuclei, the coiling that occurs during mitosis condenses the chromosomes beyond what is need for the chromosomes to fit within the nucleus. Centromeres are not formed by chromosome coiling and chromosome coiling is not what holds sister chromatids together. Instead, the coiling that occurs during mitosis is necessary to allow the chromosomes to align in the middle of the cell and then to move apart during mitosis.

Good job!

A chromatid is one copy of a newly copied chromosome that is still joined to the other copy (chromatid) by a single centromere. Therefore, there is one centromere for every pair of sister chromatids. Therefore, if there are 20 chromatids (= 10 pairs of sister chromatids), there are 10 centromeres.

Good work!

Typically the mitotic phase is divided into two, partially overlapping sub-phases. Nuclear division occurs during mitosis and division of the cytoplasm to form two daughter cells occurs during cytokinesis. So, if a cell undergoes one round of mitosis without undergoing cytokinesis, that means that the nucleus of that cell divides but the cell itself does not divide, leaving a single cell with two nuclei.

Good work!

The amount of DNA per cell doubles during S phase, as DNA synthesis (replication) takes place. The amount of DNA per cell remains at this level during G2 phase, and then is cut in half during mitosis and remains at that lower level during G1 phase. So, cells that are in G2
phase, or prophase or metaphase (sub-phases of mitosis that occur before mitosis is complete and the amount of DNA per cell decreases) have twice as much DNA as cells in G1 phase.
